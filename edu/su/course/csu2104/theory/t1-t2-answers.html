What is the goal of computer Forensics in Investigation?

The goal of computer forensics in an investigation is to collect, preserve, analyze and present digital evidence in a legally admissible manner.

  1. Identify potential evidence on computers, mobile devices, servers and cloud storage.
  2. Secure original media and create exact bit-for-bit copies, maintaining a clear chain of custody.
  3. Analyze those copies to recover active, deleted or encrypted information and reconstruct user actions.
  4. Document every tool, method and timestamped step to ensure transparency and repeatability.
  5. Produce a clear, concise report (and expert testimony if needed) that supports legal or corporate decision-making.

Define Transnational Crime.

Transnational crime is any illegal activity whose planning, execution or impact crosses national borders, exploiting differences in laws and enforcement between countries.

Key Characteristics:

  1. Cross-Border Reach: Involves people, money or data moving across two or more nations.
  2. Organized Networks: Carried out by structured groups coordinating internationally.
  3. Technology-Assisted: Uses digital tools (hacking software, encrypted messaging, darknet markets).
  4. Profit-Driven: Aimed at financial gain through fraud, trafficking or data theft.
  5. Legal Gaps: Exploits variations in legal systems, creating challenges for investigation and prosecution.

Examples:

  • A hacker ring in Country A stealing customer records from banks in Country B.
  • Trafficking stolen credit-card data via darknet markets to buyers worldwide.

Relevance to Digital Forensics:

  • Requires coordinated evidence collection from devices in multiple jurisdictions.
  • Demands strict chain-of-custody and collaboration with foreign law-enforcement agencies.

What is ‘Identity Fraud’?

Identity fraud is the unauthorized use, theft or manipulation of someone’s personal identifying information to commit financial or other crimes for gain.

Key Characteristics:

  1. Personal Data Misuse: Exploits names, government ID numbers, credit-card details.
  2. False Representation: Criminals impersonate real or synthetic identities to deceive businesses.
  3. Financial Motive: Opening accounts, obtaining loans, making purchases in victim’s name.
  4. Hidden Crime: Often goes unnoticed until unusual transactions appear.
  5. Scalable Tactics: From single-account takeovers to large-scale synthetic identity networks.

Common Methods:

  • Phishing & Social Engineering: Tricking victims into revealing credentials.
  • Data Breaches & Darknet Purchases: Acquiring stolen personal data online.
  • Card Skimming & Dumpster Diving: Capturing payment data or retrieving discarded documents.
  • Synthetic Identity Creation: Merging real and fake data to build new profiles.

Examples:

  • Fraudster uses stolen SSN to open multiple credit cards and max them out.
  • Criminal files false tax refund using another person’s identity.

Relevance to Digital Forensics:

  • Involves tracing login histories, transaction logs and device metadata.
  • Requires correlating evidence across databases, emails and network records to reconstruct the fraud chain.

What is meant by Phishing attack?

Phishing attack is a type of social-engineering fraud where an attacker impersonates a trusted entity to trick victims into revealing sensitive data or installing malware.

Key Features:

  1. Spoofed Sender: Email or message appears from banks, online services or colleagues.
  2. Malicious Link/Attachment: Directs user to a fake login page or drops harmful software.
  3. Emotional Hook: Uses urgency (“Update now!”) or fear (“Your account is locked!”).
  4. Data Theft Goal: Aims to harvest credentials, credit-card details or personal information.
  5. Mass Distribution: Sent to large recipient lists hoping for some to fall victim.

Common Variants:

  • Email Phishing: Generic bulk emails with fake invoices or alerts.
  • Spear Phishing: Tailored messages targeting specific individuals or roles.
  • Whaling: High-profile targets like CEOs or executives.
  • Smishing/Vishing: Phishing via SMS or voice calls.

Everyday Example: You get an “Account Suspended” email from “your bank” asking you to click a link and log in—but the link goes to a cloned website that steals your password.

Forensic Considerations:

  • Email Header Analysis: Trace the true origin of the message.
  • URL and Attachment Examination: Recover malicious payloads and landing pages.
  • Endpoint Artifacts: Extract browser history, registry entries and downloaded files.
  • Log Correlation: Tie victim actions to network logs and server requests.

Define the term ‘HACKING’.

Hacking is the unauthorized exploitation of weaknesses in computer systems, networks or applications to gain access, extract data or alter functionality without permission.

Key Points:

  1. Unauthorized Access: Entering systems or data without legal rights.
  2. Exploitation of Vulnerabilities: Leveraging software bugs, misconfigurations or human error.
  3. Objectives Vary: From data theft and service disruption to privilege escalation and system manipulation.
  4. Hacker Categories:
    • Black Hat: Malicious actors seeking personal gain.
    • White Hat: Security professionals testing systems with authorization.
    • Grey Hat: Operating without permission but not purely malicious.
  5. Common Techniques:
    • Brute-Force/Password Cracking
    • Social Engineering & Phishing
    • Malware Deployment
    • Exploiting Unpatched Software

Everyday Example:
A hacker uses a known vulnerability in a home router’s firmware to access its settings, intercept network traffic and steal Wi-Fi passwords.

Digital Forensics Role:

  • Analyze system logs and memory dumps to identify intrusion methods.
  • Reconstruct attacker timeline by correlating artifacts and timestamps.
  • Preserve evidence (images, logs, registry entries) for legal proceedings.

Classify different types of Computer Forensics Technology.

Computer forensics technologies are grouped by the type of data source and analysis environment:

  1. Disk/Storage Forensics
    • Examines hard drives, SSDs, USB sticks to recover files, partitions and deleted data.
  2. Memory (RAM) Forensics
    • Captures live RAM to extract running programs, network connections and encryption keys.
  3. Network Forensics
    • Captures and analyzes network traffic (packets, logs) to reconstruct intrusions and data flows.
  4. Mobile Device Forensics
    • Extracts data from smartphones and tablets: calls, messages, app data and location history.
  5. Cloud Forensics
    • Acquires and analyzes data in cloud services via snapshots, APIs and distributed logs.
  6. Email Forensics
    • Parses email headers, bodies and attachments to trace senders, recipients and tampering.
  7. Database Forensics
    • Examines database tables and transaction logs to detect unauthorized changes.
  8. Malware Forensics
    • Performs static/dynamic analysis of malicious code to uncover payloads and command-and-control links.
  9. IoT Forensics
    • Recovers and analyzes data from smart devices (cameras, sensors) with diverse storage formats.

Each type uses specialized tools (e.g., EnCase for disk, Volatility for memory, Wireshark for network) and follows strict chain-of-custody procedures.

Explain in detail the types of Cybercrime basic concepts.

  1. Unauthorized Access (Hacking): Gaining entry to systems or networks without permission by exploiting software bugs, weak passwords or misconfigurations.
    Example: Using a default router password to break into a neighbor’s Wi-Fi.

  2. Malware Attacks: Deploying malicious software—viruses, worms, trojans—to damage systems, steal data or create backdoors.
    Example: Installing a keylogger hidden inside a free game download.

  3. Ransomware: Encrypting a victim’s files and demanding payment (usually cryptocurrency) for the decryption key.
    Example: A pop-up demanding Bitcoin to unlock your documents.

  4. Phishing & Social Engineering: Trick users into revealing credentials or installing malware via fake emails, websites or calls.
    Example: An email claiming to be “your bank” asking you to reset your password on a spoofed site.

  5. Denial-of-Service (DoS/DDoS): Flooding servers or networks with excessive traffic to make services unavailable.
    Example: A botnet sending millions of requests to crash a small online store.

  6. Man-in-the-Middle (MitM): Intercepting or altering communications between two parties without their knowledge.
    Example: A rogue hotspot in a café capturing your login and payment details.

  7. SQL Injection & Web Attacks: Inserting malicious SQL or scripts into web inputs to read, modify or delete database records.
    Example: Entering ’ OR ’1’=’1 into a login form to bypass authentication.

  8. Identity Theft & Fraud: Stealing personal information (SSN, credit-card data) to impersonate someone for financial gain.
    Example: Using stolen SSN to open credit accounts in the victim’s name.

  9. Cryptojacking: Covertly using a user’s CPU/GPU to mine cryptocurrency via hidden scripts.
    Example: A website running background JavaScript that mines Monero on every visitor’s browser.

  10. Cyberstalking & Harassment: Repeatedly sending threats, abusive messages or exposing personal data to intimidate victims.
    Example: Posting private photos and harassing messages across social media accounts.

Differentiate between Cyber Stalking and Cyber Squatting.

Cyber Stalking

  • Unauthorized, repeated harassment or monitoring of a person via digital channels (social media, email, messaging).
  • Goal: intimidate, threaten or instill fear.
  • Methods: persistent unwanted messages, fake profiles, tracking online activities.
  • Example: sending hundreds of threatening emails to an ex-partner.

Cyber Squatting

  • Registering, using or selling domain names similar to established brands or personal names without rights.
  • Goal: profit from resale, mislead users or block legitimate use.
  • Methods: buying domains like “amaz0n-sale.com” or “johnsmith.org” to demand payment.
  • Example: registering “nike-shoes-discount.com” then charging Nike to transfer it.

Explain in detail, how we can crack the password of a system?

  1. Preserve Evidence:

    • Create a bit‐for‐bit copy of the target disk or RAM so the original remains untouched.
    • Log every action (who, when, what) to maintain chain of custody.

  2. Extract Password Hashes:

    • Windows: Dump NTLM hashes from the SAM file and SYSTEM registry hive (e.g., using samdump2 or bkhive).
    • Linux/Unix: Copy the /etc/shadow file containing salted password hashes.

  3. Choose Cracking Mode:

    • Offline Attack: Use extracted hashes—unlimited speed, no lockouts.
    • Online Attack: Submit guesses to a live login interface—limited by account lockout policies.

  4. Select a Cracking Tool:

    • Hashcat: GPU‐accelerated, handles large hash sets.
    • John the Ripper: Flexible rule sets, good for hybrid attacks.

  5. Run a Dictionary Attack:

    • Load common password lists (e.g., rockyou.txt).
    • The tool hashes each entry and compares it to the stolen hash.

  6. Escalate to Brute‐Force & Hybrid Attacks:

    • Brute‐Force: Try all possible combinations of letters, numbers and symbols up to a set length.
    • Hybrid/Rule‐Based: Automatically mutate dictionary words (capitalize, replace letters, append numbers).

  7. Speed Up with Rainbow Tables & GPUs:

    • Rainbow Tables: Use precomputed hash tables for instant lookups (trades disk space for time).
    • GPU Acceleration: Leverage graphics cards for parallel hash computations.

  8. Alternative Methods:

    • Live Memory Capture: If the user is logged in, dump RAM to recover plaintext credentials.
    • Social Engineering/Keylogging: (authorized scenarios) deploy scripts to record keystrokes or trick users into revealing passwords.

  9. Document & Report:

    • Record tool names, versions, commands used, start/end times and cracked results.
    • Securely archive logs and outputs as court-ready evidence.

Define Computer Forensics and explain its primary objectives.

Definition: Computer forensics is the systematic process of identifying, preserving, extracting, analyzing and presenting digital evidence from computers and related devices to support legal or investigative actions.

Primary Objectives:

  1. Identification: Locate and recognize all potential evidence on hard disks, mobile devices, cloud storage or network logs.
  2. Preservation: Secure original data by creating exact bit-for-bit copies and maintaining a documented chain of custody to prevent alteration.
  3. Analysis: Examine the preserved copies to recover active, deleted or encrypted files, reconstruct user activities and interpret timestamps.
  4. Documentation: Record every tool, method and finding in detailed, time-stamped logs to ensure transparency and reproducibility.
  5. Presentation: Produce clear, concise reports and expert testimony that explain technical findings in simple terms for judges, juries or corporate decision-makers.

What are the key challenges faced in Computer Forensics investigations?

  1. Data Volume: Modern drives and cloud accounts hold terabytes of files, emails and logs, making it hard to locate relevant evidence quickly.
  2. Encryption & Anti-Forensics: Suspects use full-disk encryption, secure messaging apps and file-wiping tools to hide or destroy data before it can be recovered.
  3. Rapid Technology Evolution: New operating systems, file formats, mobile apps and IoT devices emerge constantly, requiring investigators to update skills and tools without delay.
  4. Chain of Custody & Evidence Integrity: Every action—from imaging a drive to running analysis—must be logged and defensible in court to prove evidence wasn’t altered.
  5. Legal & Jurisdictional Hurdles: Data often resides in different states or countries, each with its own privacy laws and legal processes, complicating timely access and cooperation.
  6. Privacy & Ethical Constraints: Investigators must balance thorough evidence collection with respecting personal privacy rights and search warrant limitations.
  7. Resource & Tool Limitations: High-end forensic software, write-blockers and specialized hardware are expensive; skilled analysts are in short supply.
  8. Time Sensitivity: In cases like ransomware or insider data theft, rapid response is critical—investigators have little time for deep analysis before systems are damaged or data destroyed.

Explain the importance of Chain of Custody in digital evidence handling.

Definition:
Chain of custody is the documented, chronological record of everyone who collected, handled, transferred and analyzed digital evidence from the moment of capture until presentation in court.

Importance:

  1. Maintains Evidence Integrity: Logs every action (who, when, what), ensuring bits remain unchanged and original data is preserved.
  2. Ensures Legal Admissibility: Courts require proof that evidence wasn’t tampered with; a complete chain of custody meets that standard.
  3. Establishes Accountability: Records each custodian and transfer, preventing unauthorized access or handling.
  4. Enables Reproducibility: Detailed documentation allows independent experts to repeat procedures and verify results.
  5. Prevents Contamination: Use of sealed bags, write-blockers and logged transfers deters accidental or deliberate alteration.
  6. Builds Trust: Clear, unbroken records assure judges, juries and stakeholders that findings are reliable.

Everyday Analogy:
Like tracking a registered parcel with signature logs at each handoff, chain of custody in forensics guarantees the ‘package’ of evidence you present is exactly what was collected.

List and briefly describe the five phases of the Computer Forensics process.

  1. Identification: Spot devices, files and data sources that may hold evidence—like finding key documents in a messy desk.
  2. Preservation: Secure originals with write-blockers and sealed bags to prevent any change—like placing papers in protective sleeves.
  3. Acquisition: Make exact, bit-for-bit copies of drives, memory or logs to work on backups—like photocopying pages to keep the originals intact.
  4. Examination & Analysis: Search copies for active, deleted or hidden data, logs and metadata to reconstruct events—like reading photocopies, highlighting key facts and piecing together a timeline.
  5. Reporting/Presentation: Summarize methods, findings and timelines in a clear report (and testimony if needed) for legal or managerial use—like handing over a concise, bullet-point summary to a judge or boss.

What is the role of a Computer Forensics Investigator in a cybercrime case?

  1. Identify Evidence Sources: Locate computers, mobile devices, network logs and cloud data that may hold relevant artifacts.
  2. Preserve Data Integrity: Secure originals with write-blockers, maintain chain-of-custody, and prevent any alteration.
  3. Acquire Forensic Copies: Create bit-for-bit images of storage media and memory dumps to ensure analysis is performed on exact replicas.
  4. Analyze Digital Artifacts: Recover deleted or encrypted files, examine logs and metadata to reconstruct timelines and user actions.
  5. Document Every Step: Record tools, commands, timestamps and decisions in detailed notes for reproducibility and legal proof.
  6. Report Findings: Produce clear, concise reports outlining methods, results and timelines for law enforcement, legal teams or corporate stakeholders.
  7. Provide Expert Testimony: Explain technical findings in simple terms in court or hearings and defend the forensic process under scrutiny.
  8. Collaborate with Stakeholders: Work closely with investigators, IT personnel, legal counsel and, if needed, international agencies to coordinate evidence gathering and case strategy.

Describe in detail the different types of digital evidence and their significance in forensic investigations.

  1. Storage Media (Hard Drives, SSDs, USBs)

    • Description: All files, deleted data, partitions and slack space.
    • Significance: Recovers documents, images or hidden files; deleted items can reveal tampering.

  2. Volatile Memory (RAM Dumps)

    • Description: Running programs, open documents, encryption keys and network sessions.
    • Significance: Captures live credentials and malware in action before shutdown wipes it.

  3. Network Traffic & Logs

    • Description: Packet captures, firewall/router logs, DNS queries.
    • Significance: Reconstructs attackers’ IP addresses, data exfiltration paths and timeline of access.

  4. Email & Messaging Records

    • Description: Email headers, bodies, attachments; chat transcripts.
    • Significance: Shows communication between suspects, phishing trails or instructions for crimes.

  5. Mobile Device Data

    • Description: SMS/MMS, call histories, app data, GPS location and deleted messages.
    • Significance: Establishes suspect movements, contacts and intent at specific times.

  6. Cloud Service Artifacts

    • Description: Snapshots, access logs, sync metadata from Dropbox, AWS S3, Google Drive.
    • Significance: Tracks file uploads/downloads and user actions across jurisdictions.

  7. System & Application Logs

    • Description: Windows Event Viewer, Linux syslogs, database audit trails.
    • Significance: Verifies login attempts, privilege escalations and configuration changes.

  8. File & Document Metadata

    • Description: Creation, modification, last-access timestamps and author info.
    • Significance: Builds accurate timelines and links documents to specific users.

  9. IoT & Embedded Device Data

    • Description: Smart camera footage, sensor logs, firmware histories.
    • Significance: Provides context in physical space (e.g., door access, environmental changes).

  10. Registry & Configuration Files

    • Description: Windows Registry hives, browser profiles, application settings.
    • Significance: Reveals installed software, USB connections and user preferences.

Explain the various legal and ethical considerations in Computer Forensics, including privacy laws and admissibility of evidence.

  1. Legal Authorization & Privacy Laws

    • Must obtain valid search warrants or court orders before seizing devices or data.
    • Comply with data-protection regulations (e.g., GDPR, India’s IT Act) to respect user privacy.
    • Limit scope of collection to what is authorized—like only opening the “Documents” folder, not browsing personal photos.

  2. Chain of Custody & Evidence Integrity

    • Record who handled evidence, when and how (hand-off logs, sealed bags, write-blockers).
    • Verify image integrity with hash algorithms (MD5/SHA256) so the court can trust data hasn’t changed.

  3. Admissibility Criteria

    • Evidence must be relevant, authentic and collected by accepted forensic methods.
    • Follow standards such as ISO 27037 or Daubert/Frye rules to ensure techniques are scientifically accepted.

  4. Jurisdictional & Cross-Border Issues

    • Data in other countries may require Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties (MLATs) or international cooperation.
    • Respect local privacy laws—what’s legal in one country may be illegal in another.

  5. Ethical Principles

    • Maintain impartiality: do not alter evidence or favor one side.
    • Protect confidentiality: share findings only with authorized parties.
    • Avoid conflicts of interest: don’t investigate cases where you have personal involvement.

  6. Professional Standards & Accreditation

    • Use certified tools and follow lab accreditation (ISO 17025) to strengthen credibility.
    • Keep skills current through continuing education and adhere to a professional code of conduct.

Discuss the major steps involved in the forensic investigation process, providing real-world examples.

  1. Identification

    • Locate devices, accounts and data sources that may hold evidence.
    • Example: Investigators identify a suspect’s seized laptop, smartphone and corporate cloud storage as potential evidence.

  2. Preservation

    • Secure originals using write-blockers, sealed evidence bags and hash verification to prevent alteration.
    • Example: A forensics team images a hard drive with a hardware write-blocker and records MD5/SHA256 hashes before analysis.

  3. Acquisition

    • Create exact, bit-for-bit copies of storage media and volatile memory.
    • Example: Using dd on Linux, analysts clone a USB stick; with DumpIt, they capture a live RAM snapshot from a running PC.

  4. Examination & Analysis

    • Recover active, deleted or hidden files; parse logs; reconstruct user actions and timelines.
    • Example: An examiner uses Volatility to extract malicious processes from a RAM dump, and Autopsy to carve deleted emails from disk images.

  5. Reporting & Presentation

    • Document every tool, command, timestamp and finding; produce a clear report and, if needed, expert testimony.
    • Example: The forensic expert delivers a bullet-point report showing timestamps of file creation, then explains the chain of events in court.

How does data acquisition work in Computer Forensics? Compare and contrast live and static acquisition techniques.

Data Acquisition Process:

  1. Preparation: Secure the scene, isolate the system and document every step for chain of custody.
  2. Duplication:
    • Create exact, bit-for-bit copies of target media (storage or memory).
    • Use write-blockers for disks or specialized tools for live memory capture.
  3. Verification: Compute and record hash values (MD5/SHA256) on original and copy to prove integrity.
  4. Documentation: Log device details, tool names, commands used and timestamps for legal defensibility.

Live Acquisition:

  • Definition: Capturing data from a running system without shutting it down.
  • Data Captured: RAM contents, active processes, open network connections, decrypted data.
  • Advantages:
    • Retrieves volatile information lost on shutdown (encryption keys, running malware).
    • Essential when powering off would destroy evidence.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Alters system state (writes to memory and logs).
    • Harder to prove zero-change, requires meticulous documentation.

Static Acquisition:

  • Definition: Imaging storage devices after powering the system down.
  • Data Captured: Entire file system—including deleted files, slack space and unallocated space.
  • Advantages:
    • No writes to source media when using hardware write-blockers.
    • Results in a repeatable, court-defensible snapshot.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Cannot capture live data (RAM, active sessions).
    • Requires safe shutdown, which may trigger anti-forensic wipes.

Comparison:

Aspect Live Acquisition Static Acquisition
System State Powered on Powered off
Data Scope Volatile memory & live activity Non-volatile storage only
Integrity Risk Higher (system changes during capture) Lower (write-blocked imaging)
Evidence Completeness Captures current system state Captures full disk contents
Legal Admissibility Needs strict logging of changes Easier to defend with hash proofs

Explain the different types of cybercrimes and how forensic analysis helps in solving them.

  1. Unauthorized Access (Hacking)

    • Crime: Exploiting vulnerabilities to enter systems without permission.
    • Forensics: Examines system logs, event records and registry entries to trace intrusion points and reconstruct attacker actions.

  2. Malware Attacks

    • Crime: Deploying viruses, trojans or worms to damage data or steal information.
    • Forensics: Captures and isolates malware samples, uses sandbox analysis and reverse-engineering to identify payload behavior and communication with command-and-control servers.

  3. Phishing & Social Engineering

    • Crime: Tricking users into revealing credentials or installing malware via fake emails or messages.
    • Forensics: Analyzes email headers, URLs and attachments, reconstructs user clicks and traces phishing infrastructure.

  4. Ransomware

    • Crime: Encrypting files and demanding payment for decryption keys.
    • Forensics: Identifies ransomware strain from file signatures, attempts key recovery, finds entry vector and restores data from backups.

  5. Denial-of-Service (DoS/DDoS)

    • Crime: Flooding networks or servers with traffic to disrupt services.
    • Forensics: Captures packet dumps and firewall logs to pinpoint attack sources, map botnet patterns and block malicious IPs.

  6. Identity Theft & Fraud

    • Crime: Using stolen personal data (SSN, credit-card details) to open accounts or make purchases.
    • Forensics: Correlates transaction logs, device metadata and account-creation records to link fraudulent actions back to suspects.

  7. SQL Injection & Web Attacks

    • Crime: Injecting malicious queries or scripts to read, modify or delete database records.
    • Forensics: Reviews web-server and database audit logs, examines malformed inputs and reconstructs injected payloads.

  8. Cyberstalking & Harassment

    • Crime: Repeated unwanted messages, threats or online monitoring to intimidate victims.
    • Forensics: Collects chat logs, social-media artifacts and IP addresses to build a timeline of harassment and identify the perpetrator.

Discuss the role of various forensic tools used in digital investigations. Provide examples of software used for disk imaging, password cracking, and network forensics.

Digital investigations rely on specialized tools to preserve, analyze and interpret digital evidence:

  1. Disk Imaging Tools

    • Role: Create exact, bit-for-bit copies of storage media without altering the original—like photocopying a document to work on the copy.
    • Key Features: Hardware/software write-blocking; hash verification (MD5/SHA256) to prove integrity.
    • Examples:
      • EnCase Imager (GUI-based, multi-format support)
      • FTK Imager (fast, lightweight)
      • dd (Linux CLI) (flexible scripting, widely available)

  2. Password Cracking Tools

    • Role: Recover plaintext passwords from stolen hash files to unlock encrypted data or user accounts—similar to testing keys until one fits.
    • Attack Modes: Dictionary, brute-force, rule-based and GPU-accelerated hybrid attacks.
    • Examples:
      • Hashcat (high-speed GPU cracking, modern hash support)
      • John the Ripper (extensible rule sets, good for custom formats)

  3. Network Forensics Tools

    • Role: Capture and analyze network traffic to trace data exfiltration, intrusions or malicious connections—like recording a phone call to replay and understand the conversation.
    • Capabilities: Packet capture, protocol analysis, session reconstruction and timeline visualization.
    • Examples:
      • Wireshark (detailed packet inspection, powerful filters)
      • tcpdump (lightweight CLI capture)
      • NetworkMiner (extracts files, images and credentials from traffic)
