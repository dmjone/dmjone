1. Problem Statement & Logic

Problem: Given $N$ small squares of side length $A$. Form the largest possible square. Side?

Logic:

  • To form a large square, we need $k^2$ small squares.
  • We must find the largest integer $k$ such that $k^2 \le N$.
  • $k = \lfloor \sqrt{N} \rfloor$.
  • The side of the large square will be $k \times A$.

1.1 Dry Run

Example: $N=4, A=3$

  • Max perfect square $\le 4$ is 4 ($2^2$).
  • So we use $2 \times 2$ grid of squares.
  • Side length = $2 \times 3 = 6$. Output: 6.

Example: $N=5, A=3$

  • Max perfect square $\le 5$ is 4 ($2^2$). Leftover 1 square unused.
  • Side length = $2 \times 3 = 6$.

1.2 Pseudocode Solution


READ T
FOR i FROM 1 TO T:
    READ N, A
    
    k = FLOOR(SQRT(N))
    side_length = k * A
    
    PRINT side_length
  • Time Complexity: $O(1)$.
  • Space Complexity: $O(1)$.
Why do we ignore the excess squares ($N - k^2$)?

A: A square must have equal sides ($k \times k$). Any remaining squares less than needed for the next size $(k+1)^2$ cannot complete a larger square, so they are discarded.
