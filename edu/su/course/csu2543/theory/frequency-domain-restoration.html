1. Introduction to the Frequency Domain

Until now, every operation (intensity transforms, spatial filtering) worked directly on pixel values, the spatial domain. The frequency domain offers a completely different lens: instead of asking "what brightness is at position $(x,y)$?", it asks "how fast does brightness change across the image?"

The foundation is the Fourier Transform: any signal, including a 2D image, can be decomposed into a sum of sine and cosine waves of different frequencies. Each wave has a frequency, an amplitude (strength), and a phase (shift).

1.1 Frequency = Rate of Intensity Change

  • Low frequencies, slowly varying intensity regions: smooth walls, gradual gradients, the sky, uniform backgrounds. These carry the bulk image structure.
  • High frequencies, rapid intensity changes: sharp edges, fine textures, noise. These carry detail.

This is why a lowpass filter (passes lows, blocks highs) smooths/blurs, and a highpass filter (passes highs, blocks lows) sharpens. The spatial-domain smoothing and sharpening filters from previous topics have exact frequency-domain counterparts.

2. The 2D Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)

The 2D DFT is the mathematical engine that converts an image from a grid of pixel intensities (spatial domain) into a map of frequency components (frequency domain). Every pixel in the frequency-domain output represents a specific 2D sinusoidal pattern present in the image.

2.1 Two Components of the Transform

At each frequency $(u,v)$, the DFT produces a complex number. From it, two quantities are extracted:

  • Magnitude (spectrum), the amplitude of that frequency component. How strong is this particular sinusoidal pattern in the image?
  • Phase angle, the spatial shift of that component. Where in the image is this pattern positioned?

For visualization, the magnitude spectrum is what you typically display. The phase, though visually uninterpretable, carries most of the structural information, swapping the phase of two images swaps their appearance.

2.2 The DC Term

The frequency $(u,v) = (0,0)$ is called the DC (zero-frequency) term. It represents a constant offset, the average intensity of the entire image. It dominates the spectrum (its value is proportional to $M \times N \times \text{mean intensity}$), which is why a log transform is applied before displaying the spectrum to compress this large value and reveal the rest.

2.3 Centering the Spectrum

By default, the DFT places the DC term at the corners. For intuitive visualization and filtering, the spectrum is shifted so that DC sits at the center. Low frequencies cluster near the center; high frequencies spread toward the edges.

# Computing and displaying the DFT
import numpy as np

# Compute 2D DFT
F = np.fft.fft2(image)
# Shift DC to center
F_shifted = np.fft.fftshift(F)
# Magnitude spectrum (log-transformed for display)
spectrum = np.log(1 + np.abs(F_shifted))

3. Frequency Domain Filtering Steps

Filtering in the frequency domain follows a fixed 5-step pipeline:

  • Step 1, Pad the image: Extend the image (typically to the next power of 2, or double the size) using zero/mirror padding. This prevents circular convolution artifacts from the DFT's inherent periodicity.
  • Step 2, Compute the 2D DFT: Transform the padded image into the frequency domain.
  • Step 3, Center the spectrum: Shift the zero-frequency component to the center of the frequency rectangle.
  • Step 4, Multiply by a filter function: Element-wise multiplication of the shifted DFT with a filter $H(u,v)$. This is where the actual filtering happens, corresponding to convolution in the spatial domain.
  • Step 5, Inverse shift and inverse DFT: Undo the centering, compute the inverse 2D DFT, discard the padded region, and take the real part as the output image.

The key insight: multiplication in frequency domain = convolution in spatial domain. Multiplying by $H(u,v)$ in frequency space is equivalent to convolving the image with the spatial-domain version of $H$.

# Frequency domain filtering pipeline
import numpy as np

# Step 1: Pad
padded = np.zeros((2*M, 2*N))
padded[:M, :N] = image

# Step 2-3: DFT and center
F = np.fft.fftshift(np.fft.fft2(padded))

# Step 4: Multiply by filter H(u,v)
G = F * H  # H is the frequency-domain filter

# Step 5: Inverse
result = np.real(np.fft.ifft2(np.fft.ifftshift(G)))
output = result[:M, :N]  # crop to original size

4. Image Smoothing: Lowpass Filters in Frequency Domain

Edges and sharp transitions produce high-frequency content. To smooth an image, attenuate the high frequencies while preserving the low ones. Three common lowpass filter profiles:

4.1 Ideal Lowpass Filter (ILPF)

Passes all frequencies within a cutoff radius $D_0$ perfectly (multiplied by 1) and completely blocks everything outside (multiplied by 0). The transition is a sharp step. While theoretically simple, this abrupt cutoff causes ringing artifacts (ripples near edges) in the output, the spatial-domain equivalent of the sinc function's oscillations.

4.2 Butterworth Lowpass Filter (BLPF)

Uses a smooth, gradual transition controlled by the filter order $n$. Higher order → steeper transition → closer to ideal (but more ringing). Lower order → gentler roll-off → less ringing. A good practical compromise between smoothing strength and artifact control.

4.3 Gaussian Lowpass Filter (GLPF)

The transition follows a Gaussian curve. No ringing artifacts at all because the Gaussian has no sharp discontinuities. The smoothest possible transition. Its spatial-domain equivalent is also a Gaussian, the only function that is its own Fourier transform. This makes it the most well-behaved filter for smoothing.

5. Image Sharpening: Highpass Filters in Frequency Domain

The exact complement of lowpass filtering. A highpass filter attenuates low frequencies (smooth regions) while preserving high frequencies (edges, detail). The same three profiles exist: Ideal, Butterworth, and Gaussian highpass filters.

The relationship is straightforward:

$$H_{\text{HP}}(u,v) = 1 - H_{\text{LP}}(u,v)$$

Subtract the lowpass filter from 1 (an all-pass filter) to get the corresponding highpass filter.

Side effect: Because the DC term (average intensity) is a low-frequency component, highpass filtering removes or attenuates it. This darkens the overall image tone. The detail is enhanced but the image may appear unnaturally dark. To fix this, the DC component can be added back after filtering.

6. The Image Degradation/Restoration Model

Image enhancement (previous topics) is subjective, you adjust contrast or sharpen until it "looks good." Image restoration is objective, you know (or estimate) what went wrong and try to mathematically reverse it.

6.1 The Degradation Model

An original image $f(x,y)$ passes through a degradation process and produces an observed (degraded) image $g(x,y)$:

$$g(x,y) = h(x,y) * f(x,y) + \eta(x,y)$$

In the frequency domain:

$$G(u,v) = H(u,v) \cdot F(u,v) + N(u,v)$$
  • $H$, the degradation function (e.g., motion blur, atmospheric turbulence, lens defocus). Known or estimated.
  • $\eta$ / $N$, additive noise (random, unpredictable).
  • $f$ / $F$, the unknown original image we want to recover.
  • $g$ / $G$, the degraded image we observe.

The restoration goal: given $g$ (observed), $H$ (known/estimated), and statistical knowledge of $\eta$, find $\hat{f}$, the best estimate of the original $f$.

7. Noise Models

Noise is any random variation in pixel values that is not part of the actual scene. It arises from two primary sources:

  • Acquisition: Sensor imperfections, thermal fluctuations, photon counting errors, environmental interference during capture.
  • Transmission: Channel interference, bit errors, electromagnetic disturbances during data transfer.

7.1 White Noise

When the Fourier spectrum of the noise is constant across all frequencies, the noise is called white noise, by analogy with white light containing all visible frequencies equally. White noise contaminates every frequency band equally.

7.2 Common Noise Types

Noise Type Character Typical Source
Gaussian Bell-shaped distribution; most common model Electronic sensor noise, thermal noise
Rayleigh Skewed distribution; always positive Range imaging, radar
Erlang (Gamma) Skewed; generalizes exponential Laser imaging
Exponential Special case of Erlang Laser imaging
Uniform Equal probability across a range Quantization noise, simulation
Salt-and-pepper (Impulse) Random pure-black and pure-white pixels Sensor malfunction, bit errors

Each noise type has a distinct histogram shape. Salt-and-pepper is unique: it produces isolated extreme pixels (0 or $L-1$) rather than gradual intensity shifts.

8. Restoration in the Presence of Noise Only

When the degradation is only additive noise (no blur, no motion, just $g = f + \eta$), spatial filtering is the most effective restoration method.

8.1 Mean Filters

  • Arithmetic mean: Standard averaging. Smooths noise but blurs edges.
  • Geometric mean: Product of pixel values raised to the power $1/mn$. Less blurring than arithmetic mean, but cannot handle zero-valued pixels.

8.2 Order-Statistic Filters

The median filter is the primary tool for removing salt-and-pepper noise. It replaces the center pixel with the median of the neighborhood, effectively ignoring the extreme outlier values (the "salt" and "pepper" pixels). Multiple passes of a median filter can progressively clean heavily corrupted images while preserving edges better than any mean filter.

9. Periodic Noise Reduction

Some degradation sources (e.g., electrical interference, scanning artifacts) produce periodic noise, a repeating pattern superimposed on the image. In the spatial domain this looks like faint stripes or grids. In the frequency domain, periodic noise appears as distinct spikes at specific frequencies, easy to identify and remove.

9.1 Bandreject Filters

Block a specific band (range) of frequencies while passing everything else. If the periodic noise occupies a known frequency band, a bandreject filter centered on that band eliminates it.

9.2 Notch Filters

More surgical than bandreject. A notch filter blocks frequencies in small, predefined neighborhoods around specific frequency points (the noise spikes). This removes only the noise frequencies while leaving nearly all other content intact. Notch filters always come in symmetric pairs, if you block frequency $(u_0, v_0)$, you must also block $(-u_0, -v_0)$ to maintain a real-valued output image.

10. Inverse Filtering and Wiener Filtering

10.1 Inverse Filtering

The simplest restoration approach. Given $G(u,v) = H(u,v) \cdot F(u,v) + N(u,v)$, estimate $F$ by dividing:

$$\hat{F}(u,v) = \frac{G(u,v)}{H(u,v)} = F(u,v) + \frac{N(u,v)}{H(u,v)}$$

The problem: wherever $H(u,v)$ is small (near zero), the noise term $N(u,v)/H(u,v)$ explodes. A tiny amount of noise at a frequency where the degradation function is weak gets amplified enormously, overwhelming the restoration. This makes inverse filtering highly sensitive to noise and often impractical for real images.

10.2 Wiener Filtering (Minimum Mean Square Error)

Wiener filtering fixes the noise-explosion problem by incorporating statistical knowledge of both the noise and the original image. Instead of blindly dividing by $H$, it minimizes the mean square error between the estimate $\hat{f}$ and the true image $f$.

The Wiener filter balances two competing goals: undoing the degradation $H$ (which requires dividing by $H$) and suppressing noise (which requires not dividing by $H$ when it's small). At frequencies where $H$ is large (strong signal, little noise amplification), it behaves like inverse filtering. At frequencies where $H$ is small (noise would dominate), it damps the output to suppress noise. The result is a much more robust restoration than inverse filtering.
