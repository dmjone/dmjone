Digital Image Processing

2026, March 8

1. What is a Digital Image?

Open any spreadsheet app on your phone right now, if you want. It has rows, columns, and each cell holds a number. A digital image is exactly that: a rectangular grid of numbers. Each cell in this grid is called a pixel (picture element). The pixel's row and column give its position, and the number stored in it tells you the brightness (intensity) at that point. That selfie you took this morning? It's just a massive spreadsheet your screen renders as color.

1.1 The Mathematical View

Mathematically, an image is a two-dimensional function f(x, y):

  • x, y = spatial coordinates (position on the plane)
  • f = amplitude at that position = intensity (brightness)

When x, y, and f are all finite and discrete (whole numbers, not continuous), the image is called a digital image. The field of processing these images using computers is Digital Image Processing (DIP).

1.2 Pixel: The Atomic Unit

A pixel is the smallest addressable element of a digital image. It has exactly two properties:

  • Location: its (x, y) coordinate in the grid
  • Value: its intensity (e.g., 0 = black, 255 = white in an 8-bit grayscale image)

A digital image of size M × N contains exactly M × N pixels, arranged in M rows and N columns.

2. Levels of Image Processing

Image processing tasks are classified into three levels based on what goes in and what comes out:

2.1 Low-Level Processing

  • Input: Image → Output: Image
  • The computer works pixel-by-pixel to clean up the image. No understanding happens here.
  • Examples: Noise reduction, contrast enhancement, image sharpening

2.2 Mid-Level Processing

  • Input: Image → Output: Attributes (edges, contours, object identities)
  • The computer extracts facts from the image: “there is an edge here," “this region is separate from that one."
  • Examples: Segmentation (splitting image into regions), object classification

2.3 High-Level Processing

  • Input: Attributes → Output: Understanding / Meaning
  • The computer interprets extracted information to make sense of the scene.
  • Examples: Scene understanding (“this is a road with cars"), autonomous navigation

Key pattern: As we go from low → mid → high, the input becomes more abstract and the output becomes more meaningful. Low-level is mechanical, high-level is intelligent.

Connecting the Dots: Sections 1 & 2 A digital image is a grid of numbers. Processing it means transforming those numbers at three levels: pixel cleanup (low), fact extraction (mid), and scene understanding (high). Every concept that follows builds on these two foundational ideas.

3. Origins of Digital Image Processing

DIP did not start with computers. It evolved through practical necessity:

  • 1920s, Newspaper Industry: The Bartlane cable system transmitted images across the Atlantic Ocean. Transmission time dropped from over a week to under 3 hours. Images used just 5 gray levels (later 15).
  • 1960s, Space Race: Images from space probes arrived with severe distortions. Scientists used computers to enhance and restore them. This was the birth of modern DIP.
  • 1970s, Medical Imaging: CT scanning allowed computers to reconstruct internal body structures from multiple projections. This proved a fundamental truth: machines can reveal visual information that humans cannot directly perceive.
A Century of Progress From newspaper wires in the 1920s (5 gray levels, 3-hour Atlantic crossing) to space probes in the 1960s to CT scans in the 1970s, DIP was born from necessity, not theory. Each breakthrough solved a real problem: faster transmission, clearer space photos, and seeing inside the human body. Today you carry more image processing power in your phone than all three eras combined.

4. Sources of Digital Images

The principal source of images is the electromagnetic (EM) energy spectrum. Different parts of this spectrum reveal different information about the world:

4.1 EM Spectrum: From Shortest to Longest Wavelength

Core insight: Shorter wavelength = higher energy = penetrates deeper. Longer wavelength = lower energy = sees surface/heat/large structures.

4.2 Non-EM Sources

  • Acoustic / Ultrasound: Sound waves reflect off tissue boundaries. Used in pregnancy scans, industrial testing. Frequency range: 20 kHz – 200 MHz.
  • Electron Microscopy: Electron beams image at nanometer scale.
  • Computer-Generated: Synthetic images (3D models, fractals), requiring no physical sensor.

Unifying idea: Regardless of source, every imaging system follows the same pattern: some form of energy interacts with a scene, a sensor captures the result, and a computer digitizes it.

5. The Fundamental Pipeline of Image Processing

When solving any DIP problem, we follow a structured sequence. Think of it as an assembly line where each station does one job, and the output feeds the next:

5.1 Acquisition

Capture energy from the scene using a sensor and digitize it. This is always step one. A poor acquisition limits everything downstream; you cannot recover information that was never captured.

5.2 Enhancement vs. Restoration

Both improve image quality, but they are fundamentally different:

AspectEnhancementRestoration
GoalMake the image “look better" to a humanRecover the original image from a known degradation
ApproachSubjective, no mathematical model of degradationObjective, uses a mathematical model of what went wrong
ExampleBrightening a dark photo, increasing contrastRemoving motion blur using the blur kernel, denoising with noise model
Success measure"Does it look better?" (human judgment)"Is it closer to the original?" (mathematical error)

5.3 Morphological Processing

Operates on the shape of objects in the image. Uses structural elements to extract boundaries, skeletons, and shape components. Think of it as a cookie-cutter approach: you press a small shape pattern against the image to extract or modify structural features.

5.4 Segmentation

Partitions the image into meaningful regions, separating “car" from “background," or “tumor" from “healthy tissue." This is widely considered the hardest step because:

  • Objects vary in appearance, size, orientation
  • Objects may overlap (occlusion)
  • Boundaries may be unclear

Automated recognition fails if segmentation is inaccurate.

5.5 Recognition

The final step: assign a label to each segmented object (“this region is a vehicle," “this is a tumor"). Uses features extracted in previous stages to classify objects into categories.

Pipeline summary: Acquisition → Enhancement/Restoration → Morphological Processing → Segmentation → Recognition

Segmentation is always called the hardest step. Know the three reasons why: object variability, occlusion, and ambiguous boundaries.

6. Components of an Image Processing System

A complete DIP system has these interconnected components:

  • Image Sensors: Convert energy (light, X-rays, etc.) into electrical signals. The front door of the system.
  • Specialized Hardware: Dedicated processors for operations that need real-time speed (e.g., digitization, arithmetic on pixel arrays).
  • Computer: The central hub, a general-purpose processor that runs all processing algorithms.
  • Image Processing Software: The algorithms, from simple filters to complex recognition pipelines.
  • Mass Storage: Images are data-heavy (a single 1024×1024 8-bit image = 1 MB). Fast, large storage is essential.
  • Image Displays: Monitors to visualize results. Quality of display affects interpretation.
  • Network / Cloud: For transmitting and remotely processing images.
  • Hardcopy: Printed output (films, prints).

7. Human Visual Perception

Understanding how humans see is essential for DIP because the end consumer of most processed images is a human eye.

7.1 Structure of the Human Eye

Key structures: Cornea (front window) → Iris (controls light entry) → Lens (focuses light) → Retina (the sensor, where light receptors live).

7.2 Two Types of Receptors on the Retina

PropertyConesRods
Count6–7 million75–150 million

Sensitive toColor + fine detailLow light (no color)
Vision typePhotopic (bright light)Scotopic (dim light)

Why this matters for DIP: In bright conditions, humans detect fine detail and color (cones). In dim conditions, they detect movement and shapes but not color (rods). Image processing parameters (like gray levels and resolution) are designed around these biological limits.

7.3 How the Eye Focuses: Key Difference from Cameras

  • Camera: Fixed focal length lens. Focuses by moving the lens closer/farther from the sensor.
  • Human Eye: Fixed distance between lens and retina (~17 mm). Focuses by changing the shape of the lens (focal length varies from 14–17 mm). This is called accommodation.

8. Image Sensing and Acquisition

Every image is born from three things working together:

  1. Illumination source: Something emits energy (sun, X-ray tube, ultrasound transducer, lamp)
  2. Scene: The energy hits objects, which reflect, absorb, or transmit it
  3. Sensor: Captures the returning energy and converts it to an electrical signal

8.1 Types of Sensors

  • Single sensor: One sensing element (e.g., photodiode). Must scan to build a full image.
  • Line sensor: A row of sensors. Captures one line at a time (e.g., flatbed scanner).
  • Array sensor: A 2D grid of sensors. Captures the entire image at once (e.g., CCD/CMOS in a camera).

After sensing, the continuous electrical signal is sampled (discretized in space) and quantized (discretized in intensity) to produce a digital image.

9. Image Formation Model

What you see in an image is the product of two independent physical phenomena:

  • Illumination i(x, y): How much energy falls on the scene at point (x, y). Determined by the light source.
  • Reflectance r(x, y): What fraction of that energy bounces back from the object at (x, y). Determined by the object's material and surface.

9.1 The Fundamental Equation

f(x, y) = i(x, y) × r(x, y)

Observed intensity = illumination × reflectance

9.2 Constraints

  • 0 ≤ i(x, y) < ∞: illumination is non-negative (can range from zero to very bright)
  • 0 ≤ r(x, y) ≤ 1: reflectance is a fraction (0 = absorbs all light [black], 1 = reflects all light [perfect mirror])
  • Therefore: 0 ≤ f(x, y) < ∞

9.3 Practical Insight

Same object under different lighting → different image (i changes). Same lighting on different objects → different image (r changes). This is why DIP separates illumination from reflectance when analyzing images.

Special case: In X-ray imaging, objects transmit rather than reflect energy. Here we use transmissivity instead of reflectance.

Critical Formula The equation f(x, y) = i(x, y) × r(x, y) is the single most important formula in this chapter. It explains why the same white shirt looks different under sunlight vs. fluorescent light (illumination changes), and why a mirror vs. velvet look different under the same lamp (reflectance changes). DIP techniques like homomorphic filtering exploit this separation.

10. Sampling and Quantization

The real world is continuous. Computers work with discrete numbers. Converting a continuous image into a digital image requires two steps:

10.1 Sampling (Discretizing Space)

Sampling decides where to measure. We place a grid of M rows and N columns over the continuous image and read intensity values only at grid points.

  • Coordinates become integers: x = 0, 1, 2, …, M−1 and y = 0, 1, 2, …, N−1
  • More samples (larger M, N) = finer grid = more spatial detail captured
  • Fewer samples = coarser grid = loss of fine detail

10.2 Quantization (Discretizing Intensity)

Quantization decides how precisely to record each measurement. Continuous intensity values are rounded to the nearest level from a fixed set.

  • Number of intensity levels: L = 2k, where k = number of bits per pixel
  • 8-bit image: L = 28 = 256 levels (0 to 255)
  • 4-bit image: L = 24 = 16 levels
  • 1-bit image: L = 21 = 2 levels (binary, black or white only)

10.3 Matrix Representation

A digital image f(x, y) of size M × N is stored as a matrix:

         [ f(0,0)     f(0,1)     ...  f(0,N-1)   ]
f(x,y) = [ f(1,0)     f(1,1)     ...  f(1,N-1)   ]
         [  ⋮          ⋮         ▿      ⋮        ]
         [ f(M-1,0)   f(M-1,1)   ...  f(M-1,N-1) ]
  • Origin (0,0) is at the top-left corner
  • x-axis goes downward (rows), y-axis goes rightward (columns)
  • Image center: (floor(M/2), floor(N/2))

10.4 Storage Size

Total bits required to store a digital image = M × N × k bits. Example: 512 × 512 image, 8 bits/pixel = 512 × 512 × 8 = 2,097,152 bits = 256 KB.

11. Spatial and Intensity Resolution

Resolution determines the quality of a digital image. There are two independent kinds:

11.1 Spatial Resolution

  • Definition: The smallest discernible detail in an image
  • Measured by: Pixels per unit distance (e.g., dots per inch / DPI)
  • Depends on: Sampling rate (number of samples, N)
  • Artifact when too low: Jagged lines (staircase edges, aliasing), where shapes look blocky

11.2 Intensity Resolution

  • Definition: The smallest discernible change in intensity level
  • Measured by: Number of bits used for quantization (k)
  • Depends on: Quantization level (L = 2k)
  • Artifact when too low: False contouring (posterization), where smooth gradients appear as abrupt bands of color

11.3 Key Comparison

AspectSpatial ResolutionIntensity Resolution
ControlsDetail / sharpness of shapesSmoothness of shading
ParameterN (number of samples)k (number of bits)
ArtifactJagged lines (aliasing)False contouring (posterization)
Sensitive toShape variationsLighting/shading variations

11.4 Iso-Preference Curves: The Tradeoff

Iso-preference curves plot combinations of N (sampling) and k (bits) that produce the same perceived quality to human observers. From these curves:

  • Images with lots of shape detail (e.g., crowd photo) → need high spatial resolution but tolerate fewer intensity levels. The brain fills in smooth shading when there's enough spatial structure.
  • Images with smooth shading (e.g., face portrait) → need high intensity resolution but tolerate lower spatial resolution. Subtle shading is what makes a face look natural.

11.5 Practical Digitization Guideline

  • Shape-critical scenes (text, crowds, architecture) → prioritize more pixels (increase N)
  • Shading-critical scenes (portraits, smooth surfaces) → prioritize more bits (increase k)

  1. Define a digital image.

    Ans: A digital image is a two-dimensional function f(x, y) where x, y (spatial coordinates) and f (intensity) are all finite, discrete quantities. It is stored as a matrix of M × N pixels, each with a position and an intensity value.

  2. What is a pixel?

    Ans: A pixel (picture element) is the smallest addressable unit of a digital image. Each pixel has two properties: a spatial location (x, y) and an intensity value representing brightness at that location.

  3. Define sampling in the context of digital image processing.

    Ans: Sampling is the process of discretizing the spatial coordinates of a continuous image. A grid of M rows and N columns is placed over the continuous image, and intensity is recorded only at grid points. This converts continuous spatial positions into discrete integer coordinates.

  4. Define quantization.

    Ans: Quantization is the process of mapping continuous intensity values to a finite set of discrete levels. The number of levels is L = 2k, where k is the number of bits per pixel. For example, 8-bit quantization gives 256 intensity levels.

  5. Differentiate between image enhancement and image restoration in one line each.

    Ans: Enhancement is subjective; it improves an image's appearance for human viewing without a mathematical degradation model (e.g., brightening a dark photo). Restoration is objective; it uses a mathematical model of the degradation to recover the original image (e.g., reversing motion blur).

Section B: 2.5 Marks Each
  1. Explain the image formation model with its equation and constraints.

    Ans: An image f(x, y) is formed by two components: illumination i(x, y), the amount of source energy falling on the scene, and reflectance r(x, y), the fraction of energy reflected by the object. The equation is: f(x, y) = i(x, y) × r(x, y). Constraints: 0 ≤ i(x, y) < ∞ (illumination is non-negative, unbounded), 0 ≤ r(x, y) ≤ 1 (reflectance is a fraction between total absorption and total reflection). Therefore 0 ≤ f(x, y) < ∞. In X-ray imaging, transmissivity replaces reflectance.

  2. Describe the three levels of image processing with one example each.

    Ans: Low-level: Input and output are both images. Example: noise reduction, where a noisy image goes in and a cleaner image comes out. Mid-level: Input is an image, output is attributes (edges, regions). Example: segmentation, partitioning a medical image into tumor vs. healthy tissue regions. High-level: Input is attributes, output is understanding. Example: autonomous navigation, where the system recognizes road, vehicles, and pedestrians to make driving decisions.

  3. Compare spatial resolution and intensity resolution.

    Ans: Spatial resolution measures the smallest perceptible spatial detail, depends on sampling rate (number of pixels per unit), and when insufficient causes jagged edges (aliasing). Intensity resolution measures the smallest discernible change in brightness, depends on bits per pixel (k), and when insufficient causes false contouring (posterization). Spatial resolution is more sensitive to shape variations; intensity resolution is more sensitive to lighting/shading variations.

  4. List and briefly describe the components of an image processing system.

    Ans: Image sensors (capture energy, convert to electrical signal), specialized hardware (dedicated processors for real-time operations), computer (central processor for all algorithms), software (processing algorithms), mass storage (archives large image data), image display (visualization of results), network/cloud (remote transmission and processing), hardcopy (printed output).

Section C: 5 Marks Each
  1. Explain the complete fundamental pipeline of image processing. Describe each step with its purpose.

    Ans: The DIP pipeline consists of five sequential steps: (1) Acquisition: A sensor captures energy from the scene and digitizes it. Quality here determines the ceiling for all subsequent processing. (2) Enhancement and Restoration: Enhancement subjectively improves appearance (contrast stretching, brightening). Restoration objectively recovers the original using a degradation model (deconvolution for blur, noise model filtering). (3) Morphological Processing: Operates on object shapes using structural elements to extract boundaries, skeletons, and connected components. Used for shape analysis before recognition. (4) Segmentation: Partitions the image into meaningful regions (e.g., separating objects from background). Methods include thresholding, edge detection, and region growing. This is the hardest step because objects vary in appearance, may be occluded, and boundaries are often ambiguous. (5) Recognition: Assigns labels to segmented objects using extracted features. This is where the system identifies “this is a car" or “this is a tumor." The pipeline flows left to right: raw data → cleaned data → structural features → regions → identified objects.

  2. Discuss sampling and quantization in detail. Include the matrix representation and storage formula.

    Ans: Sampling converts continuous spatial coordinates into a discrete M × N grid, where x = 0, 1, ..., M−1 (rows) and y = 0, 1, ..., N−1 (columns). Each grid point becomes a pixel. Higher M and N capture finer spatial detail. Quantization maps each pixel's continuous intensity to one of L = 2k discrete levels. With k = 8 bits, we get 256 levels (0 = black, 255 = white). Lower k means fewer shades, causing visible banding (false contouring). Matrix form: The image is stored as matrix A where element ai,j = f(i, j), with origin (0,0) at the top-left, x increasing downward, y increasing rightward. Center of image is at (floor(M/2), floor(N/2)). Storage: Total bits = M × N × k. Example: 1024 × 1024, 8-bit image = 1024 × 1024 × 8 = 8,388,608 bits = 1 MB. Key tradeoff: More samples and more bits improve quality but increase storage and processing cost.

Section A: 2 Marks Each
  1. What is spatial resolution?

    Ans: Spatial resolution is the size of the smallest perceptible detail in an image. It is measured by the number of pixels per unit distance and depends on the sampling rate. Higher spatial resolution means finer details are visible.

  2. What is intensity resolution?

    Ans: Intensity resolution is the smallest discernible change in intensity level. It is measured by the number of bits (k) used for quantization. Higher intensity resolution means smoother gradations between shades.

  3. Define reflectance in the image formation model.

    Ans: Reflectance r(x, y) is the fraction of illumination energy that is reflected by the object at point (x, y). It ranges from 0 (total absorption, appears black) to 1 (total reflection). It depends on the material and surface properties of the object.

  4. What are cones and rods in the human eye?

    Ans: Cones (6–7 million) are photoreceptors concentrated in the fovea, responsible for color vision and fine spatial detail in bright light (photopic vision). Rods (75–150 million) are distributed across the retina, sensitive to low light but not to color (scotopic vision).

  5. What is segmentation in DIP?

    Ans: Segmentation is the process of partitioning an image into its constituent regions or objects. For example, separating a tumor from surrounding healthy tissue in a medical image. It is the most challenging step in the DIP pipeline.

Section B: 2.5 Marks Each
  1. Explain the equation f(x,y) = i(x,y) × r(x,y) with practical significance.

    Ans: The observed intensity f(x,y) at any point is the product of illumination i(x,y) and reflectance r(x,y). Illumination depends on the light source (0 to ∞), reflectance depends on the object surface (0 to 1). Same object under different lighting produces different images (i changes). Same lighting on different objects produces different images (r changes). This decomposition is useful because many enhancement techniques manipulate i and r separately. For example, adjusting brightness changes the illumination component without affecting the underlying object appearance.

  2. What are iso-preference curves? What do they reveal?

    Ans: Iso-preference curves plot combinations of spatial resolution (N samples) and intensity resolution (k bits) that produce the same perceived image quality as judged by human observers. They reveal that: (1) Images with lots of geometric detail (e.g., crowd) maintain quality with fewer intensity levels because the spatial structure compensates. (2) Images with smooth shading (e.g., face) require more intensity levels because subtle gradations are critical. This guides digitization choices: allocate bits vs. pixels based on image content.

  3. Describe the image acquisition process from scene to digital image.

    Ans: Step 1: An illumination source emits energy toward the scene. Step 2: The scene objects reflect, absorb, or transmit this energy depending on their material properties. Step 3: A sensor (single element, line array, or 2D array) captures the returning energy and converts it into a continuous electrical signal. Step 4: The continuous signal is sampled (spatial discretization into M × N grid) and quantized (intensity discretization into L = 2k levels) to produce the final digital image matrix.

  4. What is morphological processing and why is it important?

    Ans: Morphological processing operates on the shape (morphology) of objects in an image. It uses small structural elements (probes) to extract shape-related features such as boundaries, skeletons, and connected components. It is important because: (1) it provides structural information needed before recognition, (2) it can separate touching objects, (3) it fills holes and removes noise based on shape, and (4) it bridges the gap between pixel-level processing and object-level understanding.

Section C: 5 Marks Each
  1. Compare spatial and intensity resolution in detail. Discuss their artifacts, sensitivity, and the iso-preference curve tradeoff.

    Ans: Spatial resolution measures the smallest perceptible spatial detail, controlled by sampling (N). When too low, shapes become blocky with jagged edges (aliasing). It is more sensitive to shape variations. Intensity resolution measures the smallest discernible change in brightness, controlled by bit depth (k). When too low, smooth gradients break into visible bands called false contouring (posterization). It is more sensitive to lighting/shading variations. Both are digitization-dependent but independent — you can have high spatial with low intensity or vice versa. Iso-preference curves show N-k combinations yielding equal perceived quality. Key findings: Shape-heavy images (crowd, text) tolerate fewer bits because rich spatial structure compensates for coarse shading; their curves lie lower on the k-axis. Shade-heavy images (faces, smooth surfaces) need more bits because subtle tonal differences define their quality; their curves lie higher on the k-axis. Practical rule: For text/crowd scenes, invest in more pixels. For portraits/gradients, invest in more bits per pixel.

  2. Explain the three levels of image processing in detail with at least two examples for each level.

    Ans: Low-Level Processing (Image → Image): Both input and output are images. The process improves image quality without extracting any meaning. Examples: (1) Noise reduction, averaging neighboring pixels to smooth out random noise from the sensor. (2) Contrast enhancement, stretching the intensity range so that a washed-out image uses the full 0–255 range. (3) Image sharpening, emphasizing edges to make blurry details crisper. Mid-Level Processing (Image → Attributes): Input is an image, output is a set of features or measurements. The process identifies structure within the image. Examples: (1) Edge detection, finding boundaries where intensity changes sharply, producing an edge map. (2) Segmentation, partitioning a satellite image into land, water, and vegetation regions. (3) Object description, computing area, perimeter, or shape descriptors of identified regions. High-Level Processing (Attributes → Understanding): Input is attributes from mid-level, output is semantic understanding. The process makes human-like interpretations. Examples: (1) Scene understanding, recognizing that a scene contains a “road with three cars and a pedestrian." (2) Autonomous navigation, using recognized objects to plan a safe driving path. (3) Medical diagnosis assistance, identifying that a segmented region matches characteristics of a malignant tumor. The three levels form a hierarchy: each depends on the quality of the previous level's output.

Set 3

Section A: 2 Marks Each
  1. What is false contouring?

    Ans: False contouring (posterization) is a visual artifact that occurs when intensity resolution is too low. Smooth gradients in the original scene appear as abrupt, visible bands of distinct gray levels instead of smooth transitions. It results from using too few bits (k) for quantization.

  2. What does the formula L = 2k represent?

    Ans: L represents the number of distinct intensity levels in a digital image, and k is the number of bits used to represent each pixel's intensity. For example, k = 8 gives L = 256 levels (0 to 255), while k = 1 gives L = 2 levels (binary: black and white only).

  3. What is the fovea?

    Ans: The fovea is a small central region of the retina densely packed with cone receptors (~6–7 million). It is responsible for sharp, detailed, color vision in bright light. When we look directly at an object, its image falls on the fovea for maximum detail perception.

  4. Define image restoration.

    Ans: Image restoration is the objective process of recovering an original image that has been degraded, using a mathematical model of the degradation. For example, if an image is blurred by camera motion, the blur function (kernel) is modeled mathematically, and the process attempts to reverse this degradation. Unlike enhancement, success is measured by mathematical closeness to the original, not human judgment.

  5. Name four components of a digital image processing system.

    Ans: (1) Image sensors: capture energy and convert to electrical signal. (2) Computer: central processor for running algorithms. (3) Mass storage: stores large image data. (4) Image display: monitors for visualization of processed results.

Section B: 2.5 Marks Each
  1. Explain the sampling process with the coordinate system used in digital images.

    Ans: Sampling places a discrete grid of M rows and N columns over a continuous image. At each grid point, the intensity is recorded. Coordinates are integers: x = 0, 1, ..., M−1 (rows, increasing downward) and y = 0, 1, ..., N−1 (columns, increasing rightward). The origin (0, 0) is at the top-left corner. Each grid point becomes one pixel with value f(x, y). The image center is located at (floor(M/2), floor(N/2)). Higher M and N produce a finer grid that captures more spatial detail.

  2. How does focusing differ between the human eye and a camera?

    Ans: In a camera, the lens has a fixed focal length, and focusing is achieved by physically moving the lens closer to or farther from the image sensor. In the human eye, the distance between the lens and the retina is fixed at approximately 17 mm. Instead, the eye focuses by changing the shape of the lens itself, varying its focal length from 14 to 17 mm, a process called accommodation. Muscles around the lens squeeze it thinner for distant objects (longer focal length) or let it thicken for near objects (shorter focal length).

  3. Briefly describe the origins and evolution of digital image processing.

    Ans: DIP began in the 1920s when the newspaper industry used the Bartlane cable system to transmit images across the Atlantic, reducing time from weeks to hours using 5 (later 15) gray levels. Modern DIP began in the 1960s during the space race, when computers were used to enhance distorted images from space probes. In the 1970s, medical imaging (CT scanning) allowed computers to reconstruct internal body structures from multiple projections. This established the fundamental truth: machines can reveal visual information that humans cannot directly perceive.

  4. Explain quantization with a bit-depth example.

    Ans: Quantization maps each pixel's continuous intensity value to the nearest discrete level from a set of L = 2k values. With k = 8 bits: 256 levels (0–255), smooth grayscale gradients appear natural. With k = 4 bits: 16 levels, slight banding becomes visible. With k = 2 bits: 4 levels, severe false contouring as the image can only show 4 distinct shades. With k = 1 bit: 2 levels (binary), only black and white remain, and all shading is lost. More bits = smoother tonal representation but more storage per pixel.

Section C: 5 Marks Each
  1. Explain the image formation model comprehensively. Include the equation, constraints, practical significance, and the special case of X-ray imaging.

    Ans: An image is represented as f(x, y), a function of spatial coordinates whose value is proportional to energy from a physical source. It has two multiplicative components: Illumination i(x, y), the energy incident on the scene from the source (e.g., sunlight, lamp). Constraint: 0 ≤ i(x, y) < ∞. Reflectance r(x, y), the fraction of illumination reflected by the object surface. Constraint: 0 ≤ r(x, y) ≤ 1, where 0 means total absorption (black body) and 1 means perfect reflection (mirror). The equation: f(x, y) = i(x, y) × r(x, y), giving 0 ≤ f(x, y) < ∞. Practical significance: The same object appears different under different lighting (i changes), and different objects under same lighting look different (r changes). DIP techniques often separate these components. For example, homomorphic filtering processes the illumination and reflectance independently by working in the logarithmic domain. X-ray special case: Objects transmit rather than reflect energy. Here, transmissivity t(x, y) replaces r(x, y). Dense materials (bone) have low transmissivity (appear bright on X-ray film) while soft tissue has higher transmissivity.

  2. Describe the complete DIP pipeline with emphasis on why segmentation is the most challenging step.

    Ans: Pipeline: (1) Acquisition: Sensor captures and digitizes scene energy. Quality here sets the upper bound for all processing. (2) Enhancement (subjective improvement like contrast stretching) and Restoration (objective recovery using degradation model like deblurring). (3) Morphological Processing: Extracts structural shape information (boundaries, skeletons) using probe elements. (4) Segmentation: Partitions image into meaningful regions. (5) Recognition: Labels each region with a semantic category. Why segmentation is hardest: (a) Object variability: the same object class (e.g., “car") can appear in countless shapes, sizes, colors, and orientations. (b) Occlusion: objects overlap, hiding parts of each other, so boundaries are incomplete. (c) Ambiguous boundaries: edges may be weak, noisy, or absent where two regions have similar intensity. (d) Illumination effects: shadows and highlights create false edges or hide true ones. (e) Cascading errors: segmentation errors propagate directly to recognition; an incorrect boundary means incorrect labeling. Methods like thresholding work only for simple cases; real-world scenes require combinations of edge detection, region growing, clustering, and increasingly, deep learning.

Section A: 2 Marks Each
  1. What is photopic vision?

    Ans: Photopic vision is the vision under bright light conditions, mediated primarily by cone receptors in the retina. It provides high spatial detail and color perception. It operates when illumination is sufficient to activate cones (e.g., daylight conditions).

  2. Define image enhancement.

    Ans: Image enhancement is a subjective process that manipulates an image to make it more suitable for human viewing or machine analysis, without using a mathematical degradation model. The result is judged by “does it look better?" Examples: contrast stretching, brightness adjustment, histogram equalization.

  3. What are spatial coordinates in the context of an image?

    Ans: Spatial coordinates (x, y) define the position of each pixel in a digital image. x represents the row index (increases downward from 0 to M−1) and y represents the column index (increases rightward from 0 to N−1). The origin (0, 0) is at the top-left corner of the image.

  4. What is acquisition in DIP?

    Ans: Acquisition is the first step in the DIP pipeline where energy from the scene is captured using a sensor and converted into a digital signal through sampling and quantization. It determines the maximum quality achievable by all subsequent processing steps.

  5. State two differences between rods and cones.

    Ans: (1) Cones are sensitive to color and work in bright light (photopic), while rods are not sensitive to color and work in dim light (scotopic). (2) Cones are concentrated in the fovea (central retina, ~6–7M) providing high spatial detail, while rods are distributed across the peripheral retina (~75–150M) providing high sensitivity but low spatial detail.

Section B: 2.5 Marks Each
  1. How is a digital image represented as a matrix? Explain with notation.

    Ans: A digital image f(x, y) of size M × N is represented as a matrix A where each element ai,j = f(i, j) stores the intensity at row i, column j. The matrix has M rows (i = 0 to M−1) and N columns (j = 0 to N−1). Origin (0,0) is at the top-left. The image can be written as: A = [ai,j] where a0,0 is top-left pixel and aM−1,N−1 is bottom-right pixel. The center of the image is at position (floor(M/2), floor(N/2)).

  2. What happens when spatial resolution is progressively reduced? Describe the visual effect.

    Ans: As spatial resolution decreases (fewer pixels per unit area): At high resolution, all fine details (e.g., watch markings, numbers, fine text) are sharp and clear. At moderate reduction, edges soften slightly and very fine details begin to blur. At further reduction, details become noticeably blurred, and text becomes hard to read, edges lose definition. At very low resolution, the image becomes severely blocky/pixelated; individual large pixels are visible, and only the gross shape (e.g., circular watch face) remains recognizable. The artifact is called aliasing or jaggies, where straight edges appear as staircase patterns.

  3. How does the EM spectrum serve as the principal source of digital images?

    Ans: Different regions of the EM spectrum reveal different information. Gamma rays image nuclear activity (medical bone scans). X-rays penetrate soft tissue to reveal bones and dense structures. Ultraviolet detects surface fluorescence (crop disease). Visible light captures surface appearance and color. Infrared detects heat (thermal imaging, night vision). Microwaves see through clouds (radar terrain mapping). Radio waves, combined with magnetic fields, image soft tissue (MRI). The unifying principle: the same acquisition pipeline applies regardless of EM band; only the energy source and sensor differ.

  4. Differentiate between image enhancement and image restoration with examples.

    Ans: Enhancement is subjective, with no mathematical model of degradation used; the goal is to make the image “look better" to a human. Example: increasing contrast of a foggy photograph so features become more visible. Restoration is objective, where a mathematical model of the degradation is known and inverted. Example: if camera motion caused blur with a known motion kernel, deconvolution reverses the blur to recover the original sharp image. Enhancement success is judged by human perception; restoration success is measured by mathematical error between the recovered and original image.

Section C: 5 Marks Each
  1. Discuss sampling, quantization, and resolution tradeoffs. How do you choose optimal digitization parameters for different types of images?

    Ans: Sampling discretizes space into M × N grid points. More samples = higher spatial resolution = finer detail. Too few samples cause jagged edges (aliasing). Quantization discretizes intensity into L = 2k levels. More bits = higher intensity resolution = smoother shading. Too few bits cause false contouring (posterization). Tradeoffs: Both spatial and intensity resolution increase storage (M × N × k bits) and processing time. The iso-preference curves show that equal perceived quality can be achieved with different N-k combinations. Choosing parameters: For images dominated by fine geometric detail (text documents, crowd scenes, architectural drawings), prioritize high spatial resolution (large N) because spatial structure is critical; fewer intensity levels are tolerable since the brain fills in shading from structural cues. For images dominated by smooth shading (portraits, medical scans of soft tissue, gradient-rich scenes), prioritize high intensity resolution (large k) because subtle tonal differences carry the important information; moderate spatial resolution suffices since the shapes are simple. Balanced approach: For general-purpose imaging, 512 × 512 or 1024 × 1024 at 8 bits (256 levels) is a standard baseline that works well for most applications.

  2. Describe all components of an image processing system and explain the role of each in the processing workflow.

    Ans: (1) Image Sensors: The entry point; they convert physical energy (light, X-rays, sound) into electrical signals. Types include single sensors, line sensors, and 2D array sensors (CCD/CMOS). Quality of acquisition depends on sensor sensitivity and resolution. (2) Specialized Image Processing Hardware: Dedicated processors (ASICs, FPGAs, GPUs) that perform pixel-level arithmetic at high speed. Essential for real-time applications like video processing. (3) Computer: The central hub that runs all processing algorithms. Can be general-purpose (CPU) or parallel (GPU). All other components connect through it. (4) Image Processing Software: The algorithms that implement the DIP pipeline, from simple filters (enhancement) to complex neural networks (recognition). Organized as modular libraries. (5) Mass Storage: Images are data-intensive (a single 1024 × 1024 color image = 3 MB). Requires fast, high-capacity storage for archives and intermediate results. Includes RAM for active processing and disk/cloud for archival. (6) Image Display: Monitors and screens for visualizing results. Display resolution and color depth affect how accurately a human can interpret the output. (7) Network / Cloud: Enables remote image transmission, distributed processing, and cloud-based storage. Critical for telemedicine, satellite imaging, and collaborative analysis. (8) Hardcopy: Physical output devices (printers, film recorders) for permanent records. Used in medical imaging (X-ray films) and publishing. All components form a closed loop: sensors capture → hardware/computer process → storage archives → display shows results → network shares them.

Section B: 2.5 Marks Each
  1. Compare the artifacts produced by low spatial resolution and low intensity resolution.

    Ans: Low spatial resolution produces jagged lines (aliasing/staircase effect). Diagonal and curved edges appear as blocky steps instead of smooth lines. The image looks pixelated, and fine details are lost entirely. This occurs because too few sample points fail to capture rapid spatial changes. Low intensity resolution produces false contouring (posterization). Areas that should show smooth gradients display abrupt jumps between distinct gray levels, creating visible bands or “contour lines." This occurs because too few intensity levels cannot represent subtle brightness differences. Both degrade quality but in different ways: jaggies affect geometry, contouring affects shading.

  2. Describe the image sensing process from energy source to digital output.

    Ans: An illumination source emits energy toward the scene. Objects in the scene interact with this energy, reflecting, absorbing, or transmitting it based on their material properties. A sensor captures the modified energy. Three sensor types: single sensor (one element, must scan), line sensor (one row, captures line by line), or array sensor (2D grid, captures full image at once like CCD/CMOS). The sensor converts energy to a continuous voltage signal proportional to intensity. This analog signal is then sampled (discretized in space to M × N) and quantized (discretized in intensity to L = 2k levels), producing a digital image matrix.

  3. How do digitization choices differ for a face image versus a crowd image?

    Ans: A face image has simple geometry (smooth surfaces) but rich, subtle shading (skin tones, shadow gradients). It needs high intensity resolution (more bits per pixel) to avoid false contouring on smooth cheeks and gradual shadows, but can tolerate moderate spatial resolution since there are few fine geometric details. A crowd image has complex geometry (many small faces, bodies, fine structures) but relatively flat shading. It needs high spatial resolution (more pixels) to resolve individual people and details, but can tolerate fewer intensity levels since the detail-rich spatial structure compensates. Iso-preference curves confirm: the face curve lies higher on the k-axis, the crowd curve lies higher on the N-axis.

  4. List the five steps of the DIP pipeline with one-line purpose for each.

    Ans: (1) Acquisition: Capture scene energy via sensor and digitize it. (2) Enhancement / Restoration: Improve image quality, subjectively (enhancement) or objectively using degradation models (restoration). (3) Morphological Processing: Extract shape-based features like boundaries and skeletons using structural elements. (4) Segmentation: Partition the image into meaningful regions or objects, the hardest and most critical step. (5) Recognition: Assign semantic labels to segmented objects based on extracted features.

  1. Explain human visual perception (eye structure, cones, rods) and discuss how it influences digital image processing design decisions.

    Ans: Eye Structure: Light enters through the cornea (transparent front surface), passes through the iris (aperture control), is focused by the lens, and projects onto the retina (sensor surface at the back). The retina contains two types of photoreceptors. Cones (~6–7 million): Concentrated in the fovea (central retina). Responsible for photopic (bright light) vision with color perception and high spatial detail. Three types sense red, green, and blue wavelengths. Rods (~75–150 million): Distributed across the peripheral retina. Responsible for scotopic (dim light) vision. Extremely sensitive to light but cannot detect color and have lower spatial resolution. Focusing mechanism: Unlike cameras (which move the lens), the eye changes the lens shape (accommodation) to focus, keeping the lens-retina distance fixed at 17 mm. Influence on DIP: (1) Gray-level quantization: The number of intensity levels (L = 2k) is chosen based on the human eye's ability to distinguish shades. (2) Spatial resolution: Display and image resolution must match cone density at typical viewing distances for perceived sharpness. (3) Color models: RGB color spaces directly correspond to the three cone types.   2. Discuss spatial and intensity resolution comprehensively, including definitions, measurement, artifacts, sensitivity, and the concept of iso-preference curves with examples.

    Ans: Spatial Resolution: Definition: the smallest discernible spatial detail. Measurement: pixels per unit distance (DPI). Depends on sampling rate (N). Artifact when too low: jagged lines (aliasing/staircase edges) where diagonal and curved edges become blocky. Sensitive to shape variations; images with complex geometry need high spatial resolution. Intensity Resolution: Definition: smallest discernible change in intensity. Measurement: number of bits per pixel (k). Depends on quantization (L = 2k). Artifact when too low: false contouring (posterization) where smooth gradients become visible bands. Sensitive to lighting/shading variations; images with smooth tonal gradients need high intensity resolution. Both are digitization-dependent: Spatial depends on N, intensity depends on k. They are independent — changing one does not affect the other. Iso-preference curves: These curves plot combinations of N and k that give equal perceived quality to human observers. Three key observations: (1) Face (simple geometry, rich shading): Curve is high on k-axis. Needs more bits, tolerates fewer pixels. Reducing k causes visible contouring on smooth skin. (2) Cameraman (moderate geometry and shading): Curve falls in the middle — balanced need for both. (3) Crowd (complex geometry, flat shading): Curve is low on k-axis. Needs more pixels to resolve many small objects, tolerates fewer intensity levels because spatial structure compensates. Practical rule: Identify whether the image is shape-dominant or shade-dominant, then allocate resources accordingly.

Section A: 2 Marks Each
  1. Define the illumination component i(x, y) in the image formation model.

    Ans: Illumination i(x, y) is the amount of source energy (e.g., light) falling on the scene at point (x, y). It depends entirely on the light source, not on the objects. Its value ranges from 0 (no illumination) to infinity. It is one of two components that determine the observed image intensity: f(x, y) = i(x, y) × r(x, y).

  2. What is transmissivity? When is it used instead of reflectance?

    Ans: Transmissivity is the fraction of incident energy that passes through an object (rather than being reflected). It is used instead of reflectance in imaging modalities where energy passes through the subject, such as X-ray imaging. Dense materials like bone transmit less energy (appear bright), while soft tissue transmits more.

  3. Where is the origin (0, 0) in a digital image, and which direction do axes go?

    Ans: The origin (0, 0) is at the top-left corner of the image. The x-axis (rows) increases downward from 0 to M−1. The y-axis (columns) increases rightward from 0 to N−1. This convention differs from standard mathematical graphs where the y-axis points upward.

  4. What is an iso-preference curve?

    Ans: An iso-preference curve is a curve on an N-k graph (spatial samples vs. intensity bits) that connects all combinations of N and k that produce the same perceived image quality as judged by human observers. Different image types (face, crowd) have different iso-preference curves, reflecting their different resolution needs.

  5. How did medical imaging in the 1970s advance the field of DIP?

    Ans: In the 1970s, CT scanning enabled computers to reconstruct internal body structures from multiple X-ray projections taken at different angles. This proved that machines can reveal visual information humans cannot directly perceive, establishing DIP as essential for medical diagnosis and expanding the field beyond space applications.

Section B: 2.5 Marks Each
  1. Why is segmentation considered the hardest step in the DIP pipeline?

    Ans: Segmentation partitions an image into meaningful regions, and is hardest because: (1) Object variability: the same object type (e.g., “car") can appear in vastly different shapes, sizes, colors, and orientations. (2) Occlusion: objects overlap and hide parts of each other, producing incomplete boundaries. (3) Ambiguous edges: boundaries may be weak, blurred, or absent where adjacent regions have similar intensity. (4) Illumination effects: shadows create false edges, highlights wash out true edges. (5) Cascading impact: segmentation errors propagate directly to recognition; incorrect region boundaries lead to incorrect labels.

  2. Explain how a digital image is stored as a matrix. Include the size formula.

    Ans: A digital image of M rows and N columns is stored as matrix A of size M × N, where element ai,j = f(i, j) represents the intensity at row i, column j. Rows range from 0 to M−1 (top to bottom), columns from 0 to N−1 (left to right). Each element uses k bits to store its intensity value from L = 2k possible levels. Total storage = M × N × k bits. Example: 512 × 512 image at 8 bits/pixel = 512 × 512 × 8 = 2,097,152 bits ≈ 256 KB.

  3. State the ranges of reflectance and illumination. Why does reflectance range from 0 to 1?

    Ans: Illumination i(x, y) ranges from 0 to ∞, as there is no physical upper limit to how much energy a source can emit (though practical limits exist). Reflectance r(x, y) ranges from 0 to 1 because it represents the fraction of incident light reflected by a surface. A value of 0 means the surface absorbs all light (e.g., black velvet, ~0.01). A value of 1 means the surface reflects all light (e.g., fresh snow, ~0.93). No surface can reflect more energy than falls on it, hence the upper bound of 1.

  4. How are spatial and intensity resolution sensitive to different types of image content?

    Ans: Spatial resolution is more sensitive to shape variations; images with fine geometric detail (text, edges, many small objects) degrade rapidly when spatial sampling is reduced, as shapes become unrecognizable. Intensity resolution is more sensitive to lighting/shading variations; images with smooth gradients (skin tones, sky, soft lighting) degrade rapidly when bit depth is reduced, as smooth transitions break into visible bands. This asymmetric sensitivity is why different images benefit from different digitization strategies, as shown by iso-preference curves.

Section C: 5 Marks Each
  1. Trace the complete journey from a physical scene to a stored digital image. Cover image formation (f = i × r), sensing, sampling, and quantization.

    Ans: Step 1, Image Formation: An illumination source emits energy toward a scene. At each point (x, y), the observed intensity is f(x, y) = i(x, y) × r(x, y), where i is illumination (how much energy hits) and r is reflectance (what fraction bounces back). Constraints: 0 ≤ i < ∞, 0 ≤ r ≤ 1. Step 2, Sensing: A sensor (single element, line array, or 2D array like CCD/CMOS) converts the reflected energy into a continuous electrical signal. A 2D array sensor captures the entire image at once; each sensing element produces a voltage proportional to the energy received. Step 3, Sampling: The continuous 2D signal is discretized in space by placing an M × N grid over it. Intensity is recorded only at grid intersections. Coordinates become integers: x = 0 to M−1, y = 0 to N−1. Origin is top-left. Higher M and N capture finer spatial detail. Too few samples cause aliasing (jagged edges). Step 4, Quantization: Each sampled intensity value is mapped to the nearest discrete level from L = 2k possible levels. With k = 8, there are 256 levels. Too few levels cause false contouring. Step 5, Storage: The result is stored as an M × N matrix where element ai,j = f(i, j). Total storage = M × N × k bits. The digital image is now ready for processing through the DIP pipeline.

  2. Explain the complete DIP pipeline and describe the different methods used at each stage.

    Ans: (1) Acquisition: Capture and digitize using sensors. Methods: CCD/CMOS arrays for visible light, X-ray detectors for medical imaging, microphone arrays for ultrasound, radar antennas for SAR. Quality of acquisition limits all downstream processing. (2) Enhancement: Subjective improvement. Methods: Contrast stretching (expand intensity range), histogram equalization (redistribute gray levels evenly), spatial filtering (smoothing for noise, sharpening for edges), brightness adjustment. No degradation model used. Restoration: Objective recovery. Methods: Inverse filtering (divide in frequency domain by degradation function), Wiener filtering (optimal balance of inverse filtering and noise), constrained least-squares (regularized approach). Requires a mathematical model of what degraded the image. (3) Morphological Processing: Shape-based operations using structural elements. Methods: Erosion (shrinks objects, removes small protrusions), dilation (expands objects, fills small holes), opening (erosion then dilation, removes small objects), closing (dilation then erosion, fills small gaps), skeletonization (reduces to one-pixel-wide structure), boundary extraction. (4) Segmentation: Partition image into regions. Methods: Thresholding (simple intensity-based separation), edge detection (Sobel, Canny, to find boundaries), region growing (start from seed, expand by similarity), clustering (k-means groups similar pixels), watershed (treats image as topography). Hardest step due to object variability, occlusion, and ambiguous boundaries. (5) Recognition: Assign labels to segmented regions. Methods: Template matching (compare to known patterns), feature-based classification (measure shape descriptors, use statistical classifiers), neural networks (learn features from training data). Success depends on quality of all previous stages, especially segmentation.
