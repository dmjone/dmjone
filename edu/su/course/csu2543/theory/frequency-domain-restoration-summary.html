Frequency Domain and Image Restoration Summary

2026, May 03
View Main Article

1. Frequency = Rate of Change

Spatial domain asks "what value at $(x,y)$?". Frequency domain asks "how fast does it change?"

  • Low: smooth regions, gradients, backgrounds → bulk structure
  • High: edges, textures, noise → detail

Lowpass blurs. Highpass sharpens.

2. The 2D DFT

Each frequency $(u,v)$ → complex number with two parts:

  • Magnitude: amplitude of that pattern
  • Phase: spatial position

2.1 DC Term

$(u,v)=(0,0)$ → average intensity. Dominates spectrum.

Display: apply $\log(1+|F|)$ to compress.

2.2 Centering

fftshift → DC at center, lows in middle, highs on edges.

Implementation
F = np.fft.fftshift(np.fft.fft2(image))
spectrum = np.log(1 + np.abs(F))

3. Filtering Pipeline (5 Steps)

  • Pad (avoid wraparound)
  • 2D DFT
  • Center shift
  • Multiply by $H(u,v)$
  • Inverse shift → IDFT → crop

Master rule: multiplication in frequency = convolution in space.

4. Lowpass Filters

  • Ideal (ILPF): hard cutoff → ringing artifacts
  • Butterworth (BLPF): smooth, tunable order $n$
  • Gaussian (GLPF): smoothest, no ringing (Gaussian is its own Fourier transform)

5. Highpass Filters

$$ H_{HP}(u,v) = 1 - H_{LP}(u,v) $$

Same three profiles: Ideal, Butterworth, Gaussian.

Side effect: attenuates DC → image darkens. Add DC back to fix.

6. Enhancement vs. Restoration

  • Enhancement: subjective ("looks good")
  • Restoration: objective (reverse known degradation)

6.1 Degradation Model

Spatial: $g = h * f + \eta$

Frequency: $G = HF + N$

  • $H$: degradation (blur, motion)
  • $\eta / N$: noise
  • Goal: recover $\hat{f}$ given $g$, $H$, noise stats

7. Noise Models

White noise: spectrum constant across all frequencies.

  • Gaussian: bell-shape; sensor/thermal
  • Rayleigh, Erlang, Exponential: skewed; laser/radar
  • Uniform: quantization, simulation
  • Salt-and-pepper: isolated extremes (0 or $L-1$); sensor faults

8. Noise-Only Restoration

Use spatial filters:

  • Arithmetic mean: smooths but blurs
  • Geometric mean: less blur, fails on zeros
  • Median: best for salt-and-pepper, preserves edges

9. Periodic Noise

Spatial: faint stripes/grids. Frequency: distinct spikes.

  • Bandreject: blocks a range of frequencies
  • Notch: surgical, blocks small neighborhoods around spikes

Rule: notch filters come in symmetric pairs (block $(u_0,v_0)$ and $(-u_0,-v_0)$).

10. Inverse vs. Wiener

10.1 Inverse

$$ \hat{F} = \frac{G}{H} = F + \frac{N}{H} $$

Problem: $H \approx 0$ → noise explodes.

10.2 Wiener (MMSE)

Minimizes mean-square error using noise/signal statistics.

  • $H$ large → behaves like inverse filter
  • $H$ small → damps to suppress noise

Concept: trade deblurring against noise amplification.

11. Decision Engine (Exam Gold)

  • Smooth/blur? → Lowpass (Gaussian: smoothest)
  • Sharpen? → Highpass; add DC back
  • Random noise only? → Mean filter
  • Salt-and-pepper? → Median filter
  • Periodic noise (stripes)? → Notch / bandreject
  • Known blur, low noise? → Inverse filter
  • Known blur, real noise? → Wiener filter

Master rule: Convolution ↔ Multiplication. Pick the domain that makes the filter cheaper.
