Digital Image Generation Across the Electromagnetic Spectrum

Introduction

Digital images span the entire EM spectrum. Each spectral band has distinct wavelength, energy, and matter-interaction properties, requiring unique instruments for image acquisition and specialized DIP techniques for processing. Below, each band is discussed from gamma rays (shortest wavelength, highest energy) to radio waves (longest wavelength, lowest energy).

Band-wise Analysis

1. Gamma Rays (λ < 0.01 nm)
Instrument: PET scanner, SPECT, Gamma camera (Anger camera).
Principle: Radioactive tracers emit gamma photons. In PET, positron-electron annihilation produces two 511 keV photons detected by scintillation crystals (NaI) coupled with photomultiplier tubes. Cannot use lenses -- coded aperture masks used instead.
DIP Techniques: Poisson noise dominates due to low photon counts. Filtered back-projection and iterative reconstruction (MLEM) rebuild images from projections. Attenuation and scatter correction are mandatory. Multi-modal fusion with CT/MRI compensates poor spatial resolution (~4 mm).
2. X-Rays (0.01 - 10 nm)
Instrument: X-ray tube + flat-panel detector, CT scanner.
Principle: High-speed electrons hit tungsten target producing X-rays. These are differentially attenuated by tissue (Beer-Lambert law) -- bones absorb more, soft tissue less. CT acquires multiple angular projections and reconstructs cross-sectional slices.
DIP Techniques: CLAHE for subtle soft-tissue contrast enhancement. Dose-noise trade-off demands strong denoising. Metal artifact reduction, beam hardening correction for CT. Edge detection (Canny, Sobel) for boundary delineation.
3. Ultraviolet (10 - 400 nm)
Instrument: UV-sensitive CCD with UV-pass filters, fluorescence microscope, space UV telescopes (GALEX).
Principle: Two modes: (a) UV reflectance -- captures reflected UV, (b) fluorescence -- UV excites fluorophores which emit longer-wavelength visible light (Stokes shift). Atmosphere absorbs far-UV, so space telescopes needed below 300 nm.
DIP Techniques: Low SNR due to poor detector quantum efficiency. Flat-field correction for non-uniform response. Richardson-Lucy deconvolution for fluorescence blur removal. Spectral unmixing separates overlapping fluorophore signals. Pseudo-color mapping visualizes invisible UV data.
4. Visible Light (400 - 700 nm)
Instrument: CCD/CMOS sensors with Bayer color filter array (RGGB).
Principle: Photoelectric effect -- photons generate electron-hole pairs in silicon proportional to intensity. Bayer mosaic enables color capture. Lens focuses light via refraction.
DIP Techniques: Most mature DIP domain. Demosaicing reconstructs full color from Bayer pattern. White balance, gamma correction, color space transforms (RGB to HSI, YCbCr). Spatial filtering (Gaussian, Laplacian). Frequency domain (DFT, DCT for JPEG). Segmentation (Otsu, watershed), feature extraction (SIFT, ORB), CNN-based deep learning.
5. Infrared (700 nm - 1 mm)
Instrument: Microbolometer (uncooled), HgCdTe/InSb cooled detectors, satellite sensors (Landsat TIRS).
Principle: All objects above 0 K emit IR (Planck's blackbody law). Thermal IR (3-14 um) images temperature differences, not reflected light. Microbolometer measures resistance change from IR heating. Near-IR (0.7-1.4 um) uses reflection like visible band.
DIP Techniques: Non-uniformity correction (NUC) for pixel response variation. Dead pixel interpolation. Temperature calibration maps raw values to absolute temperature. Pseudo-coloring for visualization. 1/f and temporal noise denoising. Visible-IR fusion for surveillance. Atmospheric correction in satellite imagery.
6. Microwaves (1 mm - 1 m)
Instrument: Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR), microwave radiometer.
Principle: Active: SAR transmits microwave pulses, records backscattered echo (amplitude + phase), synthesizes large aperture from platform motion. Passive: radiometer measures emitted microwaves. Penetrates clouds, vegetation, and soil.
DIP Techniques: Speckle noise (multiplicative, not additive) needs specialized filters -- Lee, Frost, Kuan. Complex-valued data requires phase unwrapping for InSAR terrain mapping. Geocoding transforms slant-range to ground-range. Polarimetric decomposition (Pauli, Freeman-Durden) for classification. Multi-look processing reduces speckle at cost of resolution.
7. Radio Waves (λ > 1 m)
Instrument: Radio interferometer arrays (VLA, ALMA, SKA).
Principle: Single dish gives poor resolution (diffraction limit = λ/D, λ very large). Interferometry: multiple antennas at large baselines measure spatial frequency components (visibilities). Image reconstructed via inverse Fourier transform -- aperture synthesis. Resolution depends on maximum baseline length.
DIP Techniques: Incomplete u-v plane sampling causes sidelobes. CLEAN algorithm iteratively deconvolves dirty beam from dirty image. Self-calibration corrects atmospheric/instrumental phase errors. RFI excision removes corrupted data. Spectral line imaging creates frequency-channel cubes.

Short to long wavelength trend: (1) noise shifts from Poisson to Gaussian to multiplicative (speckle), (2) image formation moves from direct detection to computational inverse problems, (3) optics shift from lenses to reflectors to antenna arrays, (4) spatial resolution degrades due to diffraction, demanding aperture synthesis and deconvolution. Each band's physics dictates its processing pipeline -- DIP is fundamentally not one-size-fits-all.
