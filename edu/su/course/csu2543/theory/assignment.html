1. Introduction

Digital images are not limited to what the human eye can perceive. Imaging systems operate across the entire electromagnetic (EM) spectrum, from extremely high-energy gamma rays (wavelength less than 0.01 nm) to very low-energy radio waves (wavelength greater than 1 m). Each spectral band interacts differently with matter, and this interaction forms the physical basis for image generation. The instruments, detectors, and image processing techniques differ significantly across bands due to variations in photon energy, wavelength, penetration ability, and noise characteristics.

The EM spectrum ordered from shortest wavelength (highest energy) to longest wavelength (lowest energy) is: Gamma Rays (λ < 0.01 nm) → X-Rays (0.01–10 nm) → Ultraviolet (10–400 nm) → Visible Light (400–700 nm) → Infrared (700 nm–1 mm) → Microwaves (1 mm–1 m) → Radio Waves (λ > 1 m).

2. Spectral Bands: Instruments, Principles, and DIP Techniques

2.1 Gamma Ray Imaging

  • Wavelength: < 0.01 nm
  • Energy: > 100 keV

Primary Instrument: Gamma camera (Anger camera), PET (Positron Emission Tomography) scanner, SPECT (Single Photon Emission Computed Tomography) scanner.

Physical Principle: Gamma rays are emitted by radioactive isotopes (tracers) injected into the body or produced by nuclear reactions. In PET, a positron emitted by the tracer annihilates with an electron, producing two 511 keV gamma photons travelling in opposite directions. Scintillation detectors (e.g., NaI(Tl) crystals) convert these high-energy photons into visible light flashes, which are then detected by photomultiplier tubes (PMTs) and digitized. In astronomy, coded-aperture masks and Compton scattering telescopes are used because gamma rays cannot be focused by conventional lenses or mirrors.

DIP Techniques Required: Due to extremely low photon counts, gamma ray images suffer from high quantum noise (Poisson noise). Image reconstruction from projections is done using filtered back-projection or iterative algorithms (e.g., Maximum Likelihood Expectation Maximization, MLEM). Noise reduction requires specialized filters such as Wiener filtering and wavelet-based denoising. Attenuation correction and scatter correction are essential preprocessing steps. Spatial resolution is inherently poor (3–5 mm for PET), so super-resolution techniques and image registration with CT/MRI (multi-modal fusion) are commonly applied.

2.2 X-Ray Imaging

  • Wavelength: 0.01–10 nm
  • Energy: 100 eV–100 keV

Primary Instrument: X-ray tube with flat panel detectors or photostimulable phosphor plates (Computed Radiography), CT (Computed Tomography) scanners.

Physical Principle: X-rays are generated when high-speed electrons strike a metal target (usually tungsten) in an X-ray tube. When these X-rays pass through an object (e.g., human body), they are attenuated differentially based on tissue density and atomic number. Dense structures like bones absorb more X-rays, creating contrast. The transmitted X-rays hit a detector that converts them into a digital image. In CT, multiple X-ray projections taken from different angles around the body are mathematically reconstructed into cross-sectional slices using algorithms like filtered back-projection or algebraic reconstruction technique (ART).

DIP Techniques Required: Contrast enhancement is critical because soft tissue differences are subtle. Histogram equalization, CLAHE (Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization), and unsharp masking are standard. Noise is a concern due to the trade-off between radiation dose and image quality; lower dose means more quantum noise. Metal artifact reduction algorithms are needed in CT when metallic implants cause streak artifacts. Beam hardening correction and ring artifact removal are also essential. Edge detection techniques (Sobel, Canny) help in bone boundary delineation. For CT specifically, sinogram filtering and iterative reconstruction methods are used.

2.3 Ultraviolet (UV) Imaging

  • Wavelength: 10–400 nm
  • Energy: 3.1–124 eV

Primary Instrument: UV-sensitive CCD/CMOS cameras with UV-pass filters, fluorescence microscopes, UV telescopes (e.g., GALEX satellite).

Physical Principle: UV imaging works on two principles: (a) UV reflectance, where objects are illuminated with UV light and the reflected UV is captured, and (b) UV fluorescence, where UV excitation causes certain materials to emit visible light. In fluorescence microscopy, fluorescent dyes (fluorophores) are excited by UV photons and emit longer-wavelength visible photons (Stokes shift). UV telescopes image hot stars and galaxies by detecting UV photons using microchannel plates or UV-sensitive CCDs. Earth's atmosphere absorbs most UV below 300 nm, so space-based telescopes are needed for far-UV and extreme-UV observations.

DIP Techniques Required: UV images often have low signal-to-noise ratio because detector quantum efficiency drops in the UV range. Background subtraction is essential due to fluorescence crosstalk. Flat-field correction compensates for non-uniform detector response. In fluorescence microscopy, deconvolution techniques (Richardson-Lucy algorithm) are used to remove out-of-focus blur. Pseudo-color mapping is applied to make UV images interpretable since UV is invisible to the human eye. Spectral unmixing separates overlapping fluorescence signals from multiple fluorophores.

2.4 Visible Light Imaging

  • Wavelength: 400–700 nm
  • Energy: 1.7–3.1 eV

Primary Instrument: CCD (Charge-Coupled Device) and CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide Semiconductor) cameras with Bayer color filter arrays, optical microscopes, optical telescopes.

Physical Principle: Visible light imaging is based on the photoelectric effect. Photons in the visible range strike a silicon-based sensor. Each photon generates one or more electron-hole pairs proportional to light intensity. In CCD sensors, charge packets are shifted through the chip and read out sequentially. In CMOS sensors, each pixel has its own charge-to-voltage conversion circuitry. Color is captured using a Bayer filter mosaic (RGGB pattern) placed over the sensor. A lens system focuses the light onto the sensor based on principles of geometric optics (refraction).

DIP Techniques Required: This is the most well-developed domain of DIP. Techniques include demosaicing (Bayer interpolation to reconstruct full-color image), white balance correction, gamma correction, and color space transformations (RGB to HSI, YCbCr, Lab). Spatial filtering (smoothing via Gaussian filter, sharpening via Laplacian) is standard. Frequency domain techniques (DFT, DCT) are used for compression (JPEG) and filtering. Morphological operations, segmentation (thresholding, region growing, watershed), and feature extraction (SIFT, SURF, ORB) are core operations. Deep learning-based techniques (CNNs) are now dominant for classification, object detection, and super-resolution in this band.

2.5 Infrared (IR) Imaging

  • Wavelength: 700 nm–1 mm
  • Energy: 1.2 meV–1.7 eV

Primary Instrument: Thermal cameras using microbolometer arrays (uncooled) or HgCdTe (Mercury Cadmium Telluride) / InSb (Indium Antimonide) detectors (cooled), NIR (Near-IR) cameras, satellite-based IR sensors (e.g., Landsat TIRS).

Physical Principle: Every object with temperature above absolute zero emits infrared radiation according to Planck's blackbody radiation law. Thermal IR imaging (MWIR: 3–5 μm, LWIR: 8–14 μm) detects this self-emitted radiation, forming images based on temperature differences rather than reflected light. In a microbolometer, incident IR radiation heats a tiny resistive element, causing a change in its electrical resistance, which is measured and digitized. Near-IR imaging (700 nm–1.4 μm) works more like visible imaging, relying on reflected NIR light and using modified silicon sensors.

DIP Techniques Required: Thermal IR images have inherently lower spatial resolution compared to visible images because longer wavelengths lead to larger diffraction limits. Non-uniformity correction (NUC) is essential to compensate for pixel-to-pixel variations in detector response. Dead pixel replacement interpolates values for non-functioning detector elements. Temperature calibration maps raw detector values to absolute temperatures. Since thermal images are single-band (grayscale), pseudo-coloring (applying color maps like “jet” or “iron”) is used for visualization. Noise reduction is critical, as thermal detectors have significant 1/f noise and temporal noise. Image fusion with visible-band images is common for surveillance and remote sensing. In satellite IR imagery, atmospheric correction removes absorption effects of water vapour and CO2.

2.6 Microwave Imaging

  • Wavelength: 1 mm–1 m
  • Energy: 1.2 μeV–1.2 meV

Primary Instrument: Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR), microwave radiometers, ground-penetrating radar (GPR).

Physical Principle: Microwave imaging can be either active or passive. In active microwave imaging (SAR), the instrument transmits microwave pulses toward the target and records the backscattered signal. The image is formed by recording both amplitude and phase of the returned echo. SAR achieves high spatial resolution by synthesizing a large antenna aperture from the motion of the platform (aircraft or satellite). The resolution depends on pulse bandwidth (range resolution) and synthetic aperture length (azimuth resolution). In passive microwave imaging (radiometry), the instrument measures naturally emitted microwave radiation from the scene, which depends on the object's emissivity and temperature. Microwaves can penetrate clouds, vegetation, and soil to some extent, which is a key advantage.

DIP Techniques Required: SAR images suffer from a unique multiplicative noise called speckle, caused by coherent interference of backscattered signals. Speckle reduction requires specialized filters like Lee filter, Frost filter, Kuan filter, and refined Lee filter, which are different from the additive noise filters used in visible imaging. SAR data is complex-valued (amplitude + phase), so phase unwrapping algorithms are needed for interferometric SAR (InSAR) applications like terrain elevation mapping. Geocoding and terrain correction transform SAR images from slant-range geometry to ground-range coordinates. Polarimetric SAR (PolSAR) data requires decomposition techniques (Pauli, Freeman-Durden, Cloude-Pottier) for land cover classification. Multi-look processing averages independent looks to reduce speckle at the cost of spatial resolution.

2.7 Radio Wave Imaging

  • Wavelength: > 1 m
  • Energy: < 1.2 μeV

Primary Instrument: Radio telescopes (single-dish and interferometric arrays such as VLA, ALMA, SKA), aperture synthesis arrays.

Physical Principle: Radio telescopes collect radio frequency electromagnetic radiation emitted by astronomical sources (pulsars, quasars, galaxies, cosmic microwave background) using large parabolic dish antennas or arrays of dipole antennas. A single dish has limited angular resolution because the diffraction limit is proportional to wavelength/diameter, and radio wavelengths are very long. To overcome this, radio interferometry is used: multiple antennas separated by large baselines simultaneously observe the same source. Each antenna pair measures one spatial frequency component (a visibility) of the sky brightness distribution. The image is reconstructed by inverse Fourier transform of the collected visibilities, a process called aperture synthesis. The achievable resolution depends on the maximum baseline length.

DIP Techniques Required: The primary challenge in radio imaging is incomplete sampling of the spatial frequency plane (u-v plane), leading to sidelobes and artifacts in the reconstructed image. The CLEAN algorithm (Högbom CLEAN, Clark CLEAN, multi-scale CLEAN) iteratively deconvolves the dirty beam (point spread function) from the dirty image to produce a cleaned image. Maximum Entropy Method (MEM) is another deconvolution approach that finds the image with maximum entropy consistent with the observed data. Calibration (self-calibration) corrects for atmospheric phase errors and instrumental gains. Radio frequency interference (RFI) excision is a preprocessing step that flags and removes corrupted data. Since radio images are inherently monochromatic, pseudo-color mapping and spectral line imaging (creating image cubes across frequency channels) are used. Image mosaicing stitches together multiple pointings for wide-field imaging.

4. Overarching Observation

As we move from short-wavelength (high-energy) to long-wavelength (low-energy) regions of the EM spectrum, several trends in image processing requirements emerge:

  • Short Wavelength / High Energy (Gamma, X-Ray): Images are formed by detecting individual photon events, leading to quantum (Poisson) noise dominance and the need for statistical reconstruction methods. Challenges include low photon counts and the need for tomographic reconstruction.
  • Medium Wavelength (UV, Visible, Near-IR): Conventional optics work well, noise is approximately Gaussian, and the full classical toolbox of spatial and frequency domain image processing applies. Challenges involve optical aberrations, color processing, and standard noise models.
  • Long Wavelength / Low Energy (Thermal IR, Microwave, Radio): Diffraction-limited resolution becomes very poor, noise models become multiplicative (speckle in SAR) or require Fourier-domain treatment (radio interferometry), and image formation itself becomes a computational inverse problem rather than a direct sensing process. Challenges include speckle/multiplicative noise, low spatial resolution, complex-valued data, and Fourier-domain reconstruction.

Furthermore, as wavelength increases, the ability to use refractive optics (lenses) decreases. Visible and UV systems use glass or quartz lenses. IR systems require special materials like germanium or zinc selenide. Microwave and radio systems abandon lenses entirely in favor of dish reflectors and interferometric arrays. This fundamental physical constraint drives the radically different image formation and processing pipelines across the spectrum.

In conclusion, digital image processing is not a one-size-fits-all discipline. The choice of enhancement, restoration, reconstruction, and analysis techniques is fundamentally dictated by the physics of the spectral band in which the image was acquired. Understanding these physical foundations is essential for selecting appropriate processing algorithms and interpreting results correctly.
