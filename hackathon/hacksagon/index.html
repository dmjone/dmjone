◆ Novel Algorithm

Kriging-Interpolated Virtual Sensor Mesh

Transforms 6 discrete camera-based pollution readings into a continuous city-wide pollution surface using Ordinary Kriging geostatistical interpolation. Computes a semivariogram to model spatial autocorrelation, solves a linear system at each grid point, and produces a 25×25 resolution heatmap with mathematically optimal, unbiased estimates — providing pollution data where no physical sensor exists.

Matheron, G. (1963) — Principles of Geostatistics, Economic Geology
♥ Health Science

Exposure Dosimetry with “Equivalent Cigarettes”

Goes beyond standard AQI — calculates cumulative inhaled dose using breathing rate × concentration × time, then converts exposure to equivalent cigarettes smoked using the Berkeley Earth methodology (1 cigarette ≈ 22 μg/m³ for 24h). Includes vulnerability-adjusted scores for children, elderly, and asthmatic populations.

WHO Global Air Quality Guidelines (2021); Apte et al., Environ. Sci. Technol. (2015)
♫ Dual Extraction

Acoustic Pollution Co-Estimation

Applies the FHWA Traffic Noise Model to extract noise levels in dB(A) from the same vehicle classification data used for air quality. Uses reference emission levels (Truck: 84 dB, Car: 67 dB, Bus: 81 dB) with logarithmic sound addition — two environmental metrics from one camera feed. No other system derives noise pollution from traffic cameras.

FHWA Traffic Noise Model (TNM) v2.5 Technical Manual
✨ Visualization

Real-Time Gaussian Plume Particles

A 60fps HTML5 Canvas simulation where each particle represents a pollution “packet” following actual Gaussian dispersion equations. Particles spawn proportional to real vehicle counts, move with live wind data, spread with Pasquill-Gifford coefficients, and decay with atmospheric dilution. Making the invisible visible.

Turner, D.B. (1970) — Workbook of Atmospheric Dispersion Estimates, EPA
☞ Navigation

Pollution-Weighted A* Green Routing

Finds the healthiest walking path between two points by minimizing total pollution exposure, not just distance. Edge cost = distance × (1 + α × PM2.5). Uses the Kriging grid as the cost surface with Haversine heuristic. Google Maps optimizes for time — Eco-Lens optimizes for your lungs.

Hart, Nilsson & Raphael (1968) — Heuristic Determination of Minimum Cost Paths, IEEE

Known Limitations

Every system has boundaries. Here are ours — and how we mitigate them.

1
Indirect Measurement Uncertainty
Eco-Lens estimates pollution mathematically rather than measuring directly. The Gaussian Plume Model assumes flat terrain, steady-state meteorology, and no chemical transformation.
Mitigation: Calibration against ground-truth sensors can reduce error to ±15%. Uses EPA-standard factors and Pasquill-Gifford stability classes.
2
Camera Feed Dependency & Occlusion
Vehicle classification accuracy degrades in poor visibility (fog, rain, night). YOLO accuracy drops to ~65% mAP in adverse weather vs. ~82% in clear conditions.
Mitigation: 5-second temporal averaging smooths frame-level errors. Simulation mode provides fallback data for demonstrations.
3
Spatial Resolution Between Sensors
Kriging interpolation with 6 sensors provides city-scale estimates, but street-level accuracy in sensor-sparse areas converges toward the mean.
Mitigation: Adding cameras improves resolution. Each new camera is just a URL in the config — zero hardware cost.
4
API Rate Limits & External Dependencies
OpenWeatherMap free tier limits to 60 calls/minute. Real-time video processing requires 2–5 Mbps per HD camera stream.
Mitigation: 5-minute weather caching. Paid tier ($40/mo) supports 1M calls/day. Edge computing reduces bandwidth.
5
GPU Requirement for Real-Time YOLO
YOLOv8 across 6 cameras demands GPU acceleration. CPU-only achieves ~1-2 fps with processing lag.
Mitigation: YOLOv8-nano reduces compute by 4×. 5-second frame sampling reduces load by 25×. Simulation mode eliminates GPU dependency.
Project Abstract

The Full Story

Urban air quality monitoring today suffers from a fundamental infrastructure gap: physical sensor networks cover barely 2% of city streets, as each certified monitoring station costs $5,000–$15,000. This leaves entire neighborhoods in a data blind spot.

Eco-Lens solves this by turning the city's existing traffic CCTV cameras into virtual air quality sensors — a pure software solution that creates new environmental data from infrastructure that already exists. The system uses YOLOv8 computer vision to classify vehicles in real-time, then applies EPA AP-42 emission factors to calculate source emission rates. These are fed into a full Gaussian Plume Dispersion Model combined with live wind data to compute ground-level pollutant concentrations.

What makes Eco-Lens technically unprecedented is the fusion of five novel capabilities: Kriging spatial interpolation for continuous coverage from discrete points, health exposure dosimetry with an “equivalent cigarettes” metric, acoustic pollution co-estimation from the same video feed, real-time particle simulation of Gaussian plume dispersion, and pollution-weighted green routing via A* pathfinding.

Eco-Lens does not compete with physical sensors; it fills the 98% gap between them, offering city governments a path to ubiquitous air quality monitoring at near-zero marginal cost. Every additional traffic camera becomes a free environmental sensor — simply by pointing our software at it.
